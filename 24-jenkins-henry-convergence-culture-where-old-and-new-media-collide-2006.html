Jenkins Henry Convergence Culture Where Old And New Media Collide 2006

1. Problem Statement

  1. Jenkins, Henry - Convergence Culture: Where Old and New Media Collide (2006)

Focused Problem Statement: Framing fan fiction creation on platforms like AO3 (L1 Machine Layer) purely as examples of "participatory culture" (L3 Jenkins' Model) risks obscuring the complex power dynamics. While fans (L2) demonstrate collective intelligence engaging with canon (L0 Reality), this optimistic view (L4 Latent View) can downplay corporate control over intellectual property and the platform's own governance logics.

2. Core Model

24. Jenkins, Henry - Convergence Culture: Where Old and New Media Collide (2006)

  • MODEL: Describes "convergence culture" as a landscape where old and new media interact in complex ways, content flows across multiple platforms, industries cooperate, and audiences migrate and actively participate (e.g., fan cultures, collective intelligence), driven by both technological change and shifting cultural practices.
  • Index:
    • ◻ Old Media ←→ ◻ New Media (Collision/Interaction)
    • ◻ Technological Change / Cultural Shifts → ◻ Convergence Culture
    • ◻ Convergence Culture → ◻ Cross-Platform Content Flow
    • ◻ Convergence Culture → ◻ Industry Cooperation / Audience Migration
    • ◻ Convergence Culture → ◻ Active Audience Participation (Collective Intelligence, Fan Cultures)

4. Spatiotemporal Framework

24. Jenkins, Henry - Convergence culture: Where old and new media collide. (2006)

  • ◻ SPATIOTEMPORAL UNIVERSE: Contemporary media landscape characterized by digitization and networked communication.
    • → [Contains] → ◻ FIELD: The flow of content across multiple media platforms and the changing relationship between media producers and consumers.
      • → [Has] → ◻ DOMAIN: Media industries (film, TV, games, music, print), digital platforms, fan communities, user-generated content × Current technological and cultural shifts.
      • → [Projects] → ◻ SIGNAL: Transmedia storytelling, fan fiction/videos, online discussions, corporate strategies for audience engagement, participatory media practices. (ℝᵏ representing content flow metrics, user participation rates, cross-platform references).
      • → [Admits] → ◻ PERTURBATION: New platforms/technologies, changing copyright laws, corporate resistance/embrace of fan activities, evolving participation literacies.
    • → [Observed Through] → ◻ WINDOW: Media studies, fan studies, cultural studies, optimistic view of participatory culture.
      • → [Defines] → ◻ OBSERVATION SCALE: Focus on the intersections of technological, industrial, cultural, and social shifts driving convergence.
      • → [Delimits] → ◻ CONTEXT BOUNDARY: Emphasizes participatory potential and collective intelligence, sometimes downplaying power imbalances or the "darker side" of participation.
      • → [Generates] → ◻ SAMPLING LATTICE: Case studies of specific media franchises (Matrix, Star Wars, Harry Potter), fan communities (Survivor spoilers), political movements (Howard Dean campaign), interactive entertainment.
    • → [Manifests As] → ◻ PHENOMENON: Convergence Culture – A cultural shift where old and new media collide, content flows across platforms, media industries and audiences interact in new ways, and consumers are transformed into active participants who produce, share, and remix media content ("participatory culture," "collective intelligence"). Driven by technological change AND cultural practices.
  • ◻ ENGINE: Case Study Analysis / Synthesis of Trends.
    • → [Applies] → ◻ TRANSFORM: Identifying patterns of convergence across different case studies, highlighting the dynamics of participation, transmedia flow, and collective intelligence.
      • → [Operates On] → ◻ PATCH: Specific instances of transmedia franchises, fan activities, corporate strategies, participatory platforms.
      • → [Produces] → ◻ INVARIANT: The core concepts of convergence culture (technological, industrial, cultural, social convergence); participatory culture; collective intelligence.
      • → [Requires] → ◻ CONTRACTION: Focusing on the synergistic and participatory aspects of media interactions.
    • → [Constructs] → ◻ REPRESENTATION: A descriptive and often optimistic model of the contemporary media environment emphasizing participation and flow.
    • → [Optimizes] → ◻ RECURSION: Applying the convergence framework to diverse examples from entertainment, politics, education, etc.
      • → [Embeds] → ◻ MODEL: Theories of active audiences, fan culture, new media technologies, cultural studies.
      • → [Projects] → ◻ METRIC: Degree of participation, cross-platform flow, collaborative activity, collective problem-solving (qualitative/descriptive).
      • → [Seeks] → ◻ FIXED POINT: Recognition of convergence culture as the dominant paradigm for understanding contemporary media.
  • ◻ METAMODEL: Theory of Convergence Culture. Governs the analysis of contemporary media flows and participation. Binds Technological/Industrial Shifts DOMAIN ↔ Cultural Practices/Audience Behavior CODMAIN.
  • Distinctions & Crossings:
    • ◻ MODEL (Jenkins' Convergence Culture framework) | ◻ REALITY (Complex media landscape with conflicts, inequalities, exploitation) → ◻ MEASUREMENT ARTIFACT (The focus on participation can sometimes act as an artifact obscuring less positive realities of platform power or labor issues).
    • ◻ TIME-LIKE (Evolution of media franchises, fan engagement over time) | ◻ SPACE-LIKE (Flow of content across platforms, geographically distributed fan communities). Convergence inherently crosses time and space boundaries.
    • ◻ CONTINUOUS (Flow of information/culture) | ◻ DISCRETE (Media platforms, user contributions, franchise installments) → ◻ SAMPLING THRESHOLD (Analyzes discrete elements to understand the overall continuous flow and participation).
  • Operations: Observe interactions between media industries and audiences. Encode these using concepts like convergence, participation, collective intelligence. Entangle technological changes (FEATURE) with shifts in cultural practices (FEATURE). Tends to Forget or downplay conflict and power imbalances compared to participation.
