Focused Problem Statement:

Early perspectives on computers often viewed them merely as instrumental tools for calculation or information processing (Level 1 Model: Computer as Tool). This functionalist framing (Level 3) obscured the profound psychological impact computers have as "evocative objects," particularly during the rise of personal computing. By engaging users (especially children) in interaction that simulates thought, agency, and rule-based worlds (Level 1 Simulation), computers act as a "second self," prompting deep reflection on fundamental questions about life, mind, and identity (Level 0 Reality of Self), reshaping subjectivity in ways ignored by purely technical accounts (Level 2 Experience, Level 4 Obscuring Psychological Dimension).

1. Core Model and Index

Turkle, S. (2005). The second self: Computers and the human spirit (20th anniversary ed.). MIT Press. (Original 1984)

MODEL: Examines the psychological impact of computers on users, particularly how interacting with computers influences people's sense of self, identity, and their understanding of what it means to be human, think, and feel, exploring the computer as a "second self" or evocative object.

Index:

3. Spatiotemporal Framework

42. Turkle, Sherry - The Second Self (2005/1984)

SPATIOTEMPORAL FRAMEWORK

◻ SPATIOTEMPORAL UNIVERSE: Homes, schools, and labs in the US during the rise of personal computing (late 1970s - early 1980s, with later reflections).

→ [Contains] → ◻ FIELD: The psychological and philosophical impact of interacting with computers, particularly on children and hobbyists forming early relationships with the technology.

→ [Has] → ◻ DOMAIN: Human users (esp. children) × Personal computers/programs × Social settings of use × Psychological processes (identity, cognition, emotion) × Philosophical concepts (mind, life, self) × Time (developmental stages, historical moment of PC emergence).

→ [Projects] → ◻ SIGNAL: Users' talk about computers, programming styles, play patterns, metaphors used to describe computers, emotional attachments/frustrations, philosophical reflections prompted by computers. (ℝᵏ representing conceptual shifts, identity statements, programming styles).

→ [Admits] → ◻ PERTURBATION: Changes in computer capabilities (graphics, AI, networking), shifts in cultural attitudes towards computers, different educational approaches to computing.

→ [Observed Through] → ◻ WINDOW: Psychoanalytically informed Ethnography, Developmental Psychology, Sociology of Technology, Human-Computer Interaction (early qualitative focus).

→ [Defines] → ◻ OBSERVATION SCALE: Micro-level focus on individual subjective experiences and interpretations of interacting with computers.

→ [Delimits] → ◻ CONTEXT BOUNDARY: Centers on the computer as an "evocative object" stimulating psychological and philosophical reflection, rather than just its instrumental function.

→ [Generates] → ◻ SAMPLING LATTICE: Interviews and observations with children learning to program (Logo), adult computer hobbyists, AI researchers, analyzing their relationships and thinking about the machine.

→ [Manifests As] → ◻ PHENOMENON: The Computer as "Second Self" – Interacting with computers acts as a powerful catalyst for self-reflection and identity formation, prompting users to negotiate boundaries between human/machine, alive/not alive, thinking/not thinking, and integrating the machine into their psychological landscape.

◻ ENGINE: Psychoanalytic Ethnographic Interpretation of Human-Computer Relationship.

→ [Applies] → ◻ TRANSFORM: Analyzing user interactions and discourse not just for cognitive processes but for underlying psychological dynamics, identity work, and grappling with metaphysical questions prompted by the computer.

→ [Operates On] → ◻ PATCH: A specific user's programming style, a child's conversation about whether a computer is alive, an adult's emotional reaction to a program, a specific software interface.

→ [Produces] → ◻ INVARIANT: Identification of the computer as an evocative object triggering boundary negotiation; patterns in how different users (e.g., "hard" vs. "soft" masters) relate to the machine; the computer's role in shaping conceptions of self and mind.

→ [Requires] → ◻ CONTRACTION: Focusing on the subjective meaning and psychological resonance of the human-computer interaction.

→ [Constructs] → ◻ REPRESENTATION: Ethnographic accounts illustrating the computer's psychological impact; the concept of the "second self"; analysis of how computers challenge traditional boundaries.

→ [Optimizes] → ◻ RECURSION: How early experiences and developing relationships with computers shape individuals' ongoing sense of self, their approach to technology, and their understanding of human uniqueness.

→ [Embeds] → ◻ MODEL: Psychoanalytic theory (object relations, ego development), developmental psychology (Piaget), sociology (symbolic interactionism), anthropology (material culture).

→ [Projects] → ◻ METRIC: Depth of psychological engagement, types of boundary questions raised, influence on identity formation, diversity of relational styles (qualitative assessment).

→ [Seeks] → ◻ FIXED POINT: Understanding the profound ways computers enter into our psychological lives and reshape our understanding of ourselves and fundamental concepts.

METAMODEL

Distinctions & Crossings:

◻ MODEL (User's evolving internal model of self, mind, life, computer) | ◻ REALITY (The behavior and capabilities of the computer encountered) → ◻ MEASUREMENT ARTIFACT (User discourse, play, programming styles that reveal their attempts to reconcile their internal models with the evocative nature of the computer; the articulated sense of a "second self").

◻ TIME-LIKE (Individual psychological development; historical emergence of personal computing) | ◻ SPACE-LIKE (The specific computer interface and capabilities; the social context of use) → ◻ CAUSAL CONE (How interacting with the specific technologies available (space-like) influences psychological development and conceptions of self over time).

◻ CONTINUOUS (Flow of subjective experience and identity formation) | ◻ DISCRETE (Specific computer programs/behaviors; distinct philosophical categories like alive/not alive; defined user styles like hard/soft mastery) → ◻ SAMPLING THRESHOLD (Moments of interaction or reflection where users confront boundaries or articulate distinct concepts/identities in relation to the machine).

Operations: [Observe] people, especially children, interacting deeply with computers. [Encode] observations through a psychoanalytic and developmental lens, looking for impacts on self/concepts. [Convolve] user actions/statements (PATCH) with the computer's evocative properties (KERNEL). [Entangle] technology use with identity formation, psychological development, and philosophical reflection. [Forget] purely functional or behavioral accounts of human-computer interaction.
