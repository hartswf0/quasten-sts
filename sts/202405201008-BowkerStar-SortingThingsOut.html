Focused Problem Statement:

Classification systems embedded within critical infrastructures (e.g., medical coding like ICD) present standardized categories (Level 1 Model) as neutral representations of reality (Level 0), obscuring the immense interpretive labor, ethical choices, and political negotiations involved in their creation and application (Level 2 Use). This "infrastructural invisibility" (Level 4 Logic) naturalizes categories that may marginalize certain experiences ('residual categories') and hides the ongoing work needed to maintain the system, leading to unintended ethical consequences and reinforcing existing power structures.

1. Core Model and Index

Bowker, G., & Star, S. L. (1999). Sorting things out: Classification and its consequences. MIT Press.

MODEL: Analyzes classification systems and standards not as neutral tools but as powerful infrastructures embedded with values and politics, arguing they shape knowledge, work, identities, and possibilities, often rendering certain realities visible while making others invisible, with significant ethical and social consequences.

2. Four-Level Analysis

Illustrative Olog Analysis (ICD System):

  • Level 0: Base World

* <Reality> = <Complex, multifaceted nature of human illness & dying>

  • Level 1: Machine Layer

* <ICD Classification System> [simulates] → <Standardized, discrete Model of distinct diseases and causes of death>

* Problem: Model inherently simplifies and potentially distorts Level 0 reality.

* <ICD System> [outputs] → <Aggregated mortality statistics based on assigned codes>

* Problem: Output appears objective, hiding the interpretive work.

* <Categorical Rules> [filters] → <Complex patient narratives into single codes (or 'residual')>

* Problem: Filtering erases nuance, performs symbolic violence.

  • Level 2: Human Use Layer

* <Doctors/Coders> [interpret/steer] → <Patient conditions to fit ICD categories (invisible 'articulation work')>

* Problem: Necessary labor & judgment are obscured.

* <Epidemiologists> [use] → <Aggregated data assuming consistency and objectivity>

* <Patients> [experience] → <Being categorized, sometimes inadequately>

SPATIOTEMPORAL FRAMEWORK

◻ SPATIOTEMPORAL UNIVERSE: Modern bureaucratic and information-intensive societies.

→ [Contains] → ◻ FIELD: Systems of classification, standardization, and categorization used in science, medicine, administration, etc.

→ [Has] → ◻ DOMAIN: Information systems × Organizational work practices × Ethical & Political contexts × Time (history of classifications).

→ [Projects] → ◻ SIGNAL: Classification codes (ICD, race categories), standards documents, database structures, forms, boundary objects, categorized data, anomalies/remainders. (ℝᵏ representing category labels, assigned codes, statistical distributions based on categories).

→ [Admits] → ◻ PERTURBATION: Changes in classification schemes, political challenges to categories, emergence of 'unclassifiable' cases, technological shifts enabling new sorting.

→ [Observed Through] → ◻ WINDOW: STS Infrastructure Studies, grounded theory, ethnomethodology, historical analysis of classification.

→ [Defines] → ◻ OBSERVATION SCALE: Meso-level analysis of classification systems in practice within organizations and across communities.

→ [Delimits] → ◻ CONTEXT BOUNDARY: Focus on the work involved in classifying, the consequences of being classified, and the inherent politics/ethics, rather than just the logical structure of categories.

→ [Generates] → ◻ SAMPLING LATTICE: Case studies like the ICD, Nursing classifications (NIC, NOC), apartheid racial classification, disease registries.

→ [Manifests As] → ◻ PHENOMENON: Classification systems as powerful, consequential, yet often invisible infrastructures that shape work, knowledge, identity, and morality; the messy work of maintaining clear categories.

◻ ENGINE: Infrastructural and Ethical Analysis of Classification.

→ [Applies] → ◻ TRANSFORM: Revealing the hidden work, embedded values, and political consequences within seemingly neutral classification systems.

→ [Operates On] → ◻ PATCH: A specific act of classification, a category definition, a standard, a boundary object, or the experience of being classified.

→ [Produces] → ◻ INVARIANT: The inherent tension between categorical purity and real-world messiness; the political/ethical weight of categories; the work of articulation needed at boundaries.

→ [Requires] → ◻ CONTRACTION: Looking beyond the surface categories to the underlying assumptions, values, and work practices they entail.

→ [Constructs] → ◻ REPRESENTATION: A model of classification as performative infrastructure with ethical gravity; concepts like 'torque,' boundary objects, ontological choices in classification.

→ [Optimizes] → ◻ RECURSION: How classifications shape the world they purport to describe, creating feedback loops (e.g., categories influencing diagnosis and treatment, reinforcing the categories).

→ [Embeds] → ◻ MODEL: Symbolic interactionism, ethnomethodology, Foucault (on categorization), Latour (ANT, boundary objects), feminist ethics.

→ [Projects] → ◻ METRIC: Visibility/invisibility created by categories, ethical consequences, degree of friction/work required, justice implications (qualitative).

→ [Seeks] → ◻ FIXED POINT: Understanding the inescapable consequences and politics embedded within any act or system of classification.

METAMODEL

Distinctions & Crossings:

◻ MODEL (The formal classification system/standard) | ◻ REALITY (The complex, messy world being classified) → ◻ MEASUREMENT ARTIFACT (The categorized data; the individuals/objects molded or marginalized by the classification; the 'remainder').

◻ TIME-LIKE (Historical evolution of classification systems) | ◻ SPACE-LIKE (The structure of categories and their application across populations/domains) → ◻ CAUSAL CONE (How classification choices create path dependencies and shape future possibilities/identities).

◻ CONTINUOUS (Gradient reality, spectrum of characteristics) | ◻ DISCRETE (The distinct boxes/categories of the classification system) → ◻ SAMPLING THRESHOLD (The boundary lines drawn by the classification, forcing phenomena into discrete categories).

Operations: [Observe] the design and use of classification systems in practice (ethnography, history). [Encode] findings using concepts like boundary objects, torque, invisibility. [Convolve] specific cases (PATCH) with the classification structure (KERNEL) to analyze consequences. [Entangle] technical standards with ethical outcomes and work practices. [Forget] the notion of perfectly neutral or objective classification.
