Focused Problem Statement:

Standard models of global capitalism and supply chains (Level 1 Models) often portray a homogenizing force expanding into pristine nature or driving linear progress (Level 3 Framing). This obscures the reality of contemporary capitalism operating through "salvage accumulation": profiting from patchy, precarious landscapes ruined by prior capitalist activity (Level 0 Reality). By following the matsutake mushroom, Tsing reveals how value chains depend on contingent, multispecies collaborations (human pickers, fungi, trees, traders - Level 2 Practices) within these disturbed ecosystems, highlighting forms of precarious life and non-scalable interconnection ignored by dominant economic narratives (Level 4 Logic: Obscuring Ruin & Collaboration).

SPATIOTEMPORAL FRAMEWORK

◻ SPATIOTEMPORAL UNIVERSE: Global landscapes characterized by capitalist disturbance, ecological precarity, and multispecies entanglement (Anthropocene/Capitalocene), traced via the matsutake mushroom.

→ [Contains] → ◻ FIELD: The interconnected social, economic, ecological, and cultural practices involved in the global commodity chain of the matsutake mushroom.

→ [Has] → ◻ DOMAIN: Disturbed forest ecologies (esp. pine forests) × Human practices (picking, trading, gifting, consuming) × Mushroom biology/symbiosis × Global market dynamics × Cultural histories/meanings × Time (seasonal cycles, historical legacies of disturbance).

→ [Projects] → ◻ SIGNAL: Matsutake fruiting patterns, picker practices/stories, market prices, trade routes, scientific studies of mycorrhizae, cultural narratives about matsutake, evidence of ecological change/damage. (ℝᵏ representing harvest yields, price fluctuations, species distribution, narrative themes).

→ [Admits] → ◻ PERTURBATION: Forest fires, logging, climate change affecting habitats, shifts in market demand, changes in regulations, emergence of new picking communities or trade routes.

→ [Observed Through] → ◻ WINDOW: Multi-sited Multispecies Ethnography, Anthropology of Globalization & Capitalism, Environmental Humanities, STS (symbiosis, infrastructure).

→ [Defines] → ◻ OBSERVATION SCALE: Following the mushroom connects micro-level practices (picking techniques) to macro-level global flows and ecological/historical processes. Focus on "patches" and "interconnections."

→ [Delimits] → ◻ CONTEXT BOUNDARY: Focuses on life and value creation within disturbance and precarity, challenging narratives of pristine nature or simple capitalist expansion; highlights multispecies collaborations.

→ [Generates] → ◻ SAMPLING LATTICE: Ethnographic research in Oregon forests, Japanese markets, Finnish Lapland, Yunnan China; analysis of scientific literature on fungi; historical research on forestry and trade.

→ [Manifests As] → ◻ PHENOMENON: Precarious Survival & Salvage Accumulation – Life, value, and meaning-making persist in landscapes ruined by capitalism through contingent, collaborative, multispecies entanglements. The matsutake economy exemplifies how wealth ("salvage") is accumulated from damaged systems, requiring specific "arts of noticing" and adaptation to instability.

◻ ENGINE: Multi-sited Ethnography Following Matsutake / Analysis of Assemblages.

→ [Applies] → ◻ TRANSFORM: Using the matsutake trail as a heuristic to map and analyze interconnected but heterogeneous assemblages of human and non-human actors across global ruins, revealing patterns of salvage and precarious co-existence.

→ [Operates On] → ◻ PATCH: A specific site, practice, actor, or relationship within the matsutake world (e.g., a picker camp in Oregon, a Japanese auction, the symbiotic relation between pine and mushroom, a trader's network).

→ [Produces] → ◻ INVARIANT: Identification of principles like salvage accumulation, scalability issues, contamination as generative, importance of patches and latent commons, necessary interspecies relations.

→ [Requires] → ◻ CONTRACTION: Focusing on the specific connections and frictions revealed by following the mushroom, embracing complexity and contingency over universal laws.

→ [Constructs] → ◻ REPRESENTATION: Ethnographic narratives weaving together diverse sites/actors ; theoretical concepts ("salvage accumulation," "assemblages," "arts of noticing") for understanding life in ruins.

→ [Optimizes] → ◻ RECURSION: How the dynamics of salvage (e.g., depletion of easily accessible patches) drive further exploration and transformation of landscapes and social relations within the matsutake economy.

→ [Embeds] → ◻ MODEL: Anthropology (globalization, political ecology), STS (ANT, multispecies studies), environmental history, critical theory (Marxist influences, world-systems theory adapted).

→ [Projects] → ◻ METRIC: Diversity of entanglement, degree of precarity, effectiveness of salvage strategies, interdependence of actors, resonance of cultural meanings (qualitative ethnographic analysis).

→ [Seeks] → ◻ FIXED POINT: Not a stable state, but a richer understanding of the dynamic, patchy, collaborative, and precarious ways life and value are made in the face of capitalist and ecological devastation.
