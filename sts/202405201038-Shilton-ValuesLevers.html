Focused Problem Statement:

Technology design processes, such as agile software development (Level 1 Machine Layer/Process), often operate with a primary focus on technical functionality, efficiency, or user experience narrowly defined (Level 3 Framing). This implicitly obscures or marginalizes ethical considerations and value trade-offs (Level 0 Reality of impact). While designers may hold ethical values, the structure of standard workflows lacks concrete points ("Values Levers") for systematically raising, discussing, and integrating these values into design decisions (Level 2 Practice Gap). This leads to technologies potentially embodying unintended or harmful values, because the prevailing design logic (Level 4) treats ethics as external or secondary to core practice.

1. Core Model and Index

Shilton, K. (2012). Values levers: Building ethics into design. Science, Technology, & Human Values, 38(3).

MODEL: Proposes the concept of "values levers"—specific moments and practices within the technology design process where ethical considerations and values can be deliberately introduced and negotiated (e.g., during requirements gathering, prototyping, testing)—as a practical way to embed ethics into technology development.

Index:

  • Level 3: Reflexive Framing Layer

SPATIOTEMPORAL FRAMEWORK

◻ SPATIOTEMPORAL UNIVERSE: Technology design and development processes within organizations (e.g., software engineering, HCI).

→ [Contains] → ◻ FIELD: The practices, workflows, tools, and decision-making points involved in creating technological systems.

→ [Has] → ◻ DOMAIN: Design methodologies (agile, waterfall) × Team roles & interactions × Design artifacts (personas, prototypes, requirements) × Organizational culture × Ethical considerations × Time (project lifecycle).

→ [Projects] → ◻ SIGNAL: Design documents, meeting minutes, code comments, prototypes, usability test results, team discussions, adoption of specific design methods (e.g., value sensitive design tools). (ℝᵏ representing mentions of values, use of specific methods, design outcomes related to ethics).

→ [Admits] → ◻ PERTURBATION: Explicit introduction of ethical guidelines, adoption of new design tools/methods, changes in team composition or leadership, external pressure (regulation, public opinion).

→ [Observed Through] → ◻ WINDOW: Value Sensitive Design (VSD), Ethics of Technology, STS, Design Studies, empirical studies of design practice.

→ [Defines] → ◻ OBSERVATION SCALE: Meso-level focus on identifying specific, actionable points within existing design workflows where ethical reflection can be integrated.

→ [Delimits] → ◻ CONTEXT BOUNDARY: Focuses on practical integration of ethics into existing design processes, rather than proposing entirely new methodologies or purely abstract principles.

→ [Generates] → ◻ SAMPLING LATTICE: Identifying common design activities (requirements gathering, brainstorming, prototyping, testing) and associating specific "levers" (tools, questions, practices) with each stage.

→ [Manifests As] → ◻ PHENOMENON: "Values Levers" – Practical, identifiable points of intervention (e.g., specific questions during requirements, types of personas used, criteria in testing) within standard technology design processes where designers can consciously surface, deliberate, and incorporate ethical values.

◻ ENGINE: Framework for Practical Integration of Ethics in Design.

→ [Applies] → ◻ TRANSFORM: Translating abstract ethical values into concrete, actionable practices that fit within the rhythm and structure of existing technology design workflows.

→ [Operates On] → ◻ PATCH: A specific stage or activity within a design process (e.g., defining user personas, prioritizing features, running a usability test).

→ [Produces] → ◻ INVARIANT: Identification of recurring opportunities ("levers") for ethical intervention across different design phases; the principle of practical, situated value integration.

→ [Requires] → ◻ CONTRACTION: Focusing on the procedural aspects of design and identifying where value discussions can be pragmatically inserted.

→ [Constructs] → ◻ REPRESENTATION: The conceptual framework of "Values Levers," including examples of specific levers tied to different design stages.

→ [Optimizes] → ◻ RECURSION: How the repeated use of values levers can potentially cultivate more ethically reflexive design habits and team cultures over time.

→ [Embeds] → ◻ MODEL: Value Sensitive Design, participatory design, reflective practice, organizational studies, software engineering process models.

→ [Projects] → ◻ METRIC: Feasibility of implementation, potential impact on design decisions, fostering of ethical deliberation within teams (qualitative assessment of practicality and potential effectiveness).

→ [Seeks] → ◻ FIXED POINT: Making ethical consideration a routine, practical, and integrated part of standard technology design, rather than an afterthought or external constraint.

METAMODEL

Distinctions & Crossings:

◻ MODEL (The Values Levers framework / Specific lever techniques) | ◻ REALITY (The actual messy, time-constrained, path-dependent process of technology design within organizations) → ◻ MEASUREMENT ARTIFACT (Design outcomes, features, or documented decisions that show evidence of having been influenced by the application of values levers; changes in team discourse).

◻ TIME-LIKE (The sequential phases of the design lifecycle) | ◻ SPACE-LIKE (The structure of the design process, team roles, organizational context) → ◻ CAUSAL CONE (Identifying specific moments/practices within the design process structure (space-like) to intervene ethically as the project unfolds over time).

◻ CONTINUOUS (The ongoing flow of design decisions and trade-offs) | ◻ DISCRETE (The identified Values Levers as specific points for focused ethical deliberation) → ◻ SAMPLING THRESHOLD (The framework highlighting specific activities or moments within the continuous design flow as crucial opportunities ("levers") for discrete ethical intervention).

Operations: [Observe] technology design workflows and practices. [Encode] observations to identify potential points of ethical intervention ("levers"). [Convolve] standard design activities (PATCH) with specific lever techniques (KERNEL) to propose integration points. [Entangle] practical design methods with ethical goals. [Forget] treating ethics as separate from or external to the pragmatic realities of design work.
