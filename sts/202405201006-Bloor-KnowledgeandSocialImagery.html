Focused Problem Statement:

Traditional epistemology and philosophies of science (Level 3 Framing) employ asymmetrical explanations for belief, assuming true/rational beliefs are self-explanatory or caused by reality (Level 0 direct access implied), while only false/irrational beliefs require social explanation. This obscures the social factors (e.g., training, authority, interests, social imagery - Level 4 Logic) that causally influence the acceptance of *all* beliefs, including those considered true and rational within a community (Level 2 Use/Acceptance). The "Strong Programme" aims to correct this by demanding symmetrical, causal social explanations for all knowledge claims (Level 1 Systems of Belief).

1. Core Model and Index

Bloor, D. (1976). Knowledge and social imagery. University of Chicago Press.

MODEL: Advocates for the "Strong Programme" in the sociology of scientific knowledge (SSK), arguing that all knowledge claims, including scientific ones (true or false, rational or irrational), should be explained symmetrically using the same types of social causes (causality, impartiality, symmetry, reflexivity).

Index:

◻ Scientific Knowledge → (is subject to) → ◻ Sociological Explanation

◻ Strong Programme → (mandates) → ◻ Causality / Impartiality / Symmetry / Reflexivity

◻ Beliefs (True/False, Rational/Irrational) → (require) → ◻ Symmetrical Explanation

◻ Social Interests / Conditions → (influence/cause) → ◻ Knowledge Claims

* <Theory> [outputs] → <Explanations for observed phenomena, predictions, experimental procedures>

* <Theoretical Framework> [filters] → <Interpretation of experimental results (e.g., weight gain explained away)>

  • Level 2: Human Use Layer

* <Scientific Community> [curates] → <Acceptance based on training, authority, explanatory fit (within its context)> (Problem: Traditional view ignores these social causes for 'true' beliefs).

  • Level 3: Reflexive Framing Layer

* <Bloor (Theorist)> [models] → <Belief acceptance via Strong Programme (Causality, Impartiality, Symmetry, Reflexivity)> / [questions] → <Asymmetrical explanations in epistemology>

* <Traditional Epistemology (Framing)> [models] → <Phlogiston acceptance as 'error' needing social explanation, Oxygen acceptance as 'truth' needing only rational explanation>

  • Level 4: Latent & Systemic Evaluation

* <Asymmetry Principle (Logic)> [obscures] → <Social causes influencing acceptance of 'true'/'rational' beliefs> (Problem: Core methodological bias hides universal social influence).

* <Strong Programme (Symmetry Principle)> [reframes] → <All belief acceptance as requiring social causal explanation> / [elevates] → <Social factors (training, interests, imagery) as key causes>

SPATIOTEMPORAL FRAMEWORK

◻ SPATIOTEMPORAL UNIVERSE: The production and acceptance of knowledge claims, particularly scientific knowledge.

→ [Contains] → ◻ FIELD: Belief systems and knowledge-making practices within social groups/cultures.

→ [Has] → ◻ DOMAIN: Social structures & interests × Cognitive processes × Historical context.

→ [Projects] → ◻ SIGNAL: Accepted beliefs, scientific theories, mathematical proofs, classification systems, controversies, norms of reasoning. (ℝᵏ representing propositions, inferential rules, social validation markers).

→ [Admits] → ◻ PERTURBATION: New evidence (interpreted socially), shifts in social interests or power structures, encounters with alternative belief systems.

→ [Observed Through] → ◻ WINDOW: The "Strong Programme" in the Sociology of Scientific Knowledge (SSK).

→ [Defines] → ◻ OBSERVATION SCALE: Social/collective level of belief formation and justification, applied to scientific communities.

→ [Delimits] → ◻ CONTEXT BOUNDARY: Focuses on causes of belief, applying symmetrically to true/false, rational/irrational beliefs; initially less focus on ontological questions of reality itself.

→ [Generates] → ◻ SAMPLING LATTICE: Historical case studies of scientific controversies, mathematical developments, anthropological accounts of belief systems.

→ [Manifests As] → ◻ PHENOMENON: Knowledge (including scientific) as a social institution, shaped by social interests and imagery; belief acceptance explained causally by social factors.

◻ ENGINE: Strong Programme Causal Analysis.

→ [Applies] → ◻ TRANSFORM: Explaining the credibility and acceptance of beliefs (regardless of truth value) using symmetrical social causes (causality, impartiality, symmetry, reflexivity).

→ [Operates On] → ◻ PATCH: A specific belief, theory, or knowledge claim accepted by a community (e.g., phlogiston theory, Euclidean geometry, N-rays).

→ [Produces] → ◻ INVARIANT: The underlying social causes (interests, imagery, training, authority) that explain the belief's acceptance or rejection within that community.

→ [Requires] → ◻ CONTRACTION: Bracketing the truth/falsity or rationality/irrationality of the belief itself to focus on the social mechanisms of its adoption.

→ [Constructs] → ◻ REPRESENTATION: A sociological model of knowledge production where social factors are the primary causal explanation for belief patterns.

→ [Optimizes] → ◻ RECURSION: Reflexivity – applying the same causal analysis to sociology of knowledge itself. The way established knowledge shapes future knowledge acceptance.

→ [Embeds] → ◻ MODEL: Durkheimian sociology, Wittgenstein's ideas on language games/forms of life, critiques of traditional epistemology/philosophy of science.

→ [Projects] → ◻ METRIC: Explanatory power of the sociological account in terms of causality and symmetry (qualitative social explanation).

→ [Seeks] → ◻ FIXED POINT: A complete causal explanation for the distribution of beliefs within a society or scientific field based on social factors.

METAMODEL

Distinctions & Crossings:

◻ MODEL (Socially accepted belief/theory) | ◻ REALITY (The external world the belief purports to describe - this is bracketed) → ◻ MEASUREMENT ARTIFACT (The belief itself, seen as an artifact of social processes, not a direct reflection of reality).

◻ TIME-LIKE (Historical development of knowledge/social context) | ◻ SPACE-LIKE (Synchronic structure of social interests/cognitive norms) → ◻ CAUSAL CONE (How social conditions causally produce patterns of belief acceptance).

◻ CONTINUOUS (Range of possible beliefs/interpretations) | ◻ DISCRETE (Accepted vs. Rejected beliefs within a community) → ◻ SAMPLING THRESHOLD (Social mechanisms (training, authority) that enforce consensus and create discrete categories of belief).

Operations: [Observe] patterns of belief in communities (esp. scientific). [Encode] these patterns as explananda for sociological analysis. [Convolve] specific beliefs (PATCH) with social structures/interests (KERNEL) to identify causal links. [Entangle] knowledge content with social context. [Forget] traditional asymmetric explanations privileging truth/rationality as self-explanatory.
