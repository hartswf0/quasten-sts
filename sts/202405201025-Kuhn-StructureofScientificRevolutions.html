Reference: Kuhn, Thomas - The Structure of Scientific Revolutions (1970)

Focused Problem Statement:

Standard accounts of scientific progress (Level 3 Framing, e.g., inductivism, falsificationism) model science as a linear, cumulative enterprise smoothly approaching truth about reality (Level 0). This obscures the actual historical structure of science, characterized by long periods of "normal science" governed by a largely unquestioned "paradigm" (Level 1 Model), punctuated by revolutionary crises triggered by anomalies. The paradigm dictates acceptable problems, methods, and even perceptions (Level 2 Practice), making progress *within* a paradigm different from the non-cumulative, gestalt-shifting nature of revolutionary change (Level 4 Logic: Paradigm Dependence).

1. Core Model and Index

Kuhn, T. (1970). The structure of scientific revolutions. University of Chicago Press.

MODEL: Argues that science progresses not linearly but through periods of "normal science" governed by a dominant "paradigm," punctuated by "scientific revolutions" where anomalies accumulate, leading to a crisis and a paradigm shift, resulting in a new, often incommensurable, way of seeing the world.

Index:

◻ Science → (progresses through) → ◻ Paradigm Shifts / Revolutions

◻ Paradigm → (governs) → ◻ Normal Science (puzzle-solving)

◻ Anomalies → (can lead to) → ◻ Crisis / Revolution

◻ New Paradigm → (is often incommensurable with) → ◻ Old Paradigm

2. Four-Level Analysis

Illustrative Olog Analysis (Transition from Newtonian Physics to Relativity):

  • Level 0: Base World

* <Reality> = <Physical phenomena related to motion, gravity, light, electromagnetism>

  • Level 1: Machine Layer

* <Newtonian Paradigm (Classical Mechanics & Gravitation)> [simulates] → <Model of absolute space/time, action-at-a-distance gravity, explaining vast range of phenomena> (Problem: Success masks later anomalies).

* <Paradigm> [outputs] → <Precise predictions, solutions to 'puzzles' (Normal Science)>

* <Paradigm's Concepts/Methods> [filters] → <Interpretation of experiments (e.g., ether drift experiments interpreted initially within paradigm)> (Problem: Paradigm shapes perception of data).

  • Level 2: Human Use Layer

* <Physicists (Normal Science period)> [steer/interpret] → <Research focused on solving puzzles defined by Newtonian paradigm>

* <Physicists (Crisis period)> [struggle] → <To reconcile anomalies (e.g., Mercury's orbit, ether drift results) with paradigm>

* <Einstein/Relativists> [propose] → <New relativistic paradigm (revolutionary shift)>

  • Level 3: Reflexive Framing Layer

* <Kuhn (Theorist/Historian)> [models] → <Science via Paradigms, Normal Science, Crisis, Revolution> / [questions] → <Linear/cumulative models of progress>

* <Cumulative Models of Science (Framing)> [model] → <Relativity as simply adding to/correcting Newton>

  • Level 4: Latent & Systemic Evaluation

* <Paradigm Dependence (Logic)> [obscures] → <The non-cumulative nature of revolutions & incommensurability> (Problem: Core mechanism of scientific change misrepresented).

* <Progress Towards Truth Narrative (Disposition)> [naturalizes] → <Linear view, hiding radical shifts in worldview>

* <Kuhn's Historical Model> [reframes] → <Scientific change as fundamentally structured by community paradigms> / [elevates] → <Role of community, psychology, anomaly in change>

3. Spatiotemporal Framework

SPATIOTEMPORAL FRAMEWORK

◻ SPATIOTEMPORAL UNIVERSE: History of Western Science.

→ [Contains] → ◻ FIELD: Scientific knowledge production and community dynamics over time.

→ [Has] → ◻ DOMAIN: Scientific communities × Shared "Paradigms" (theories, methods, values, exemplars) × Anomalies × Time (periods of normal science vs. revolution).

→ [Projects] → ◻ SIGNAL: Textbooks, scientific publications, research instrumentation, documented anomalies, scientific controversies, accounts of discoveries, shifts in foundational theories. (ℝᵏ representing theoretical terms, experimental results, citation patterns).

→ [Admits] → ◻ PERTURBATION: Accumulation of anomalies that resist solution within the current paradigm, emergence of a competing paradigm, generational shifts in the scientific community.

→ [Observed Through] → ◻ WINDOW: Historical analysis of scientific change, focusing on community structures and shared assumptions (paradigms).

→ [Defines] → ◻ OBSERVATION SCALE: Macro-historical analysis of major shifts in scientific fields (e.g., Copernican revolution, chemical revolution, relativity, quantum mechanics).

→ [Delimits] → ◻ CONTEXT BOUNDARY: Focuses on the internal dynamics of scientific communities and the role of shared paradigms, less on external social/political influences (though acknowledged).

→ [Generates] → ◻ SAMPLING LATTICE: Historical case studies illustrating paradigm shifts (Ptolemy to Copernicus, phlogiston to oxygen, Newton to Einstein).

→ [Manifests As] → ◻ PHENOMENON: Scientific development characterized by periods of "Normal Science" (puzzle-solving within an accepted Paradigm) punctuated by "Scientific Revolutions" where persistent Anomalies trigger a Crisis, leading to a non-cumulative Paradigm Shift and subsequent "incommensurability" between old and new worldviews.

◻ ENGINE: Paradigm-Based Historical Analysis of Science.

→ [Applies] → ◻ TRANSFORM: Interpreting scientific history not as linear accumulation of truth, but as a sequence of distinct paradigms shaping perception, problems, and standards.

→ [Operates On] → ◻ PATCH: A specific historical episode in science (e.g., discovery of oxygen, Michelson-Morley experiment, blackbody radiation problem).

→ [Produces] → ◻ INVARIANT: Identification of the governing paradigm, the nature of normal science puzzles, the anomalies leading to crisis, the characteristics of the revolutionary shift, and evidence of incommensurability.

→ [Requires] → ◻ CONTRACTION: Focusing on the shared cognitive and normative framework (paradigm) of the scientific community in a given period.

→ [Constructs] → ◻ REPRESENTATION: The model of scientific change through paradigms, normal science, crisis, revolution, and incommensurability.

→ [Optimizes] → ◻ RECURSION: How a paradigm enables efficient normal science by defining legitimate problems and methods, but also restricts vision, eventually leading to anomalies it cannot resolve, paving the way for its own replacement.

→ [Embeds] → ◻ MODEL: History of science, sociology of knowledge (community focus), cognitive psychology (gestalt shifts as analogy), philosophy of science (critique of inductivism/falsificationism).

→ [Projects] → ◻ METRIC: Stability of normal science tradition, severity of anomalies, degree of incommensurability between paradigms, speed/nature of paradigm adoption (qualitative historical analysis).

→ [Seeks] → ◻ FIXED POINT: An understanding of the typical historical pattern of development within mature sciences, characterized by the structure of paradigms and revolutions.

METAMODEL

Distinctions & Crossings:

◻ MODEL (The Paradigm - worldview, theories, methods) | ◻ REALITY (The natural world being investigated) → ◻ MEASUREMENT ARTIFACT (Anomalies – empirical results that resist assimilation into the paradigm's model of reality; also, the specific 'world' perceived through the lens of the paradigm).

◻ TIME-LIKE (The historical sequence of normal science -> crisis -> revolution -> new normal science) | ◻ SPACE-LIKE (The structure of a paradigm defining a coherent field of puzzles and standards at a given time) → ◻ CAUSAL CONE (How the established paradigm (space-like) guides research until anomalies accumulate over time, triggering a revolutionary break).

◻ CONTINUOUS (Gradual accumulation of knowledge/anomalies) | ◻ DISCRETE (Distinct paradigms separated by revolutionary breaks; normal vs. revolutionary periods) → ◻ SAMPLING THRESHOLD (The revolutionary moment of paradigm shift, analytically separating distinct, often incommensurable, scientific worldviews).

Operations: [Observe] historical scientific texts, controversies, discoveries. [Encode] history in terms of paradigms, normal science, anomalies, crises, revolutions. [Convolve] specific findings/anomalies (PATCH) with the reigning paradigm (KERNEL) to understand their reception (puzzle vs. counterinstance). [Entangle] cognitive frameworks, community structures, and scientific change. [Forget] purely linear, cumulative models of scientific progress.
