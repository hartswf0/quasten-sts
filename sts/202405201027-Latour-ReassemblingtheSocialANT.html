Focused Problem Statement:

Traditional sociology often invokes pre-existing social structures, forces, or categories (e.g., 'class', 'society', 'gender' - Level 1 Models or Level 4 Logic) to explain human actions (Level 0 Reality). This obscures the practical, ongoing, and contingent work that heterogeneous actors (human and non-human) perform (Level 2 Practice) through processes of "translation" (redefining interests/identities) and mediation (via artifacts) to *assemble* temporary and reversible associations. ANT (Level 3 Framing) proposes shifting from explaining action *by* the social to describing *how* the social is assembled *through* these actor-network formations, thus avoiding premature explanations that hide the actual work of association.

1. Core Model and Index

Latour, B. (2008). Reassembling the social: An introduction to actor-network-theory. Oxford University Press.

MODEL: Introduces Actor-Network Theory (ANT) not as a theory of the social, but a method for tracing associations; argues the "social" should be understood as dynamic networks of heterogeneous actors (both human and non-human) constantly being assembled and reassembled through processes of translation and enrollment.

Index:

◻ Social → (is redefined as) → ◻ Tracing of Associations / Network Assembly

◻ Actor-Network Theory (ANT) → (is a method for) → ◻ Following Actors

◻ Actors → (are) → ◻ Heterogeneous (Human / Non-human)

◻ Networks → (are built through) → ◻ Translation / Enrollment

  • Level 1 Outputs: *(Traditional model outputs abstract explanation)* vs. *(ANT traces network formation/failure)*

3. Spatiotemporal Framework

27. Latour, Bruno - Reassembling the Social: An Introduction to Actor-Network-Theory (2008)

SPATIOTEMPORAL FRAMEWORK

◻ SPATIOTEMPORAL UNIVERSE: Any situation involving the formation of groups, collectives, or associations (the "social").

→ [Contains] → ◻ FIELD: The dynamic processes of association and dissociation between heterogeneous entities (human and non-human "actors" or "actants").

→ [Has] → ◻ DOMAIN: Networks of interacting actors × Mediating artifacts/technologies × Processes of translation/enrollment × Time (tracing network formation/dissolution).

→ [Projects] → ◻ SIGNAL: Actors' actions and accounts, controversies, translations (redefinitions of interests), inscriptions (traces left by action), technological artifacts mediating relations, formation/dissolution of groups. (ℝᵏ representing network links, actor types, inscription durability).

→ [Admits] → ◻ PERTURBATION: Introduction of new actors, failure of translations, breakdown of alliances, emergence of controversies, re-negotiation of networks.

→ [Observed Through] → ◻ WINDOW: Actor-Network Theory (ANT) as a methodology for tracing associations, not a theory of the social.

→ [Defines] → ◻ OBSERVATION SCALE: Focus on tracing the specific, contingent links and transformations that assemble collectives; follows the actors themselves.

→ [Delimits] → ◻ CONTEXT BOUNDARY: Rejects a priori definitions of the "social" or "society"; the boundaries of the network are empirical outcomes of the tracing process. Includes non-humans as actors symmetrically.

→ [Generates] → ◻ SAMPLING LATTICE: Following actors through controversies, observing processes of negotiation/translation, analyzing mediating objects/inscriptions (e.g., scientific papers, technologies, organizational charts).

→ [Manifests As] → ◻ PHENOMENON: The "Social" not as a pre-existing domain or structure, but as an ongoing, practical achievement of "assembling" heterogeneous networks through actor interactions, translations, and mediations.

◻ ENGINE: ANT Methodology / Following the Actors.

→ [Applies] → ◻ TRANSFORM: Shifting focus from explaining action by invoking "social forces" to describing the practical work actors do to create associations, enroll others, and stabilize networks. Symmetrical treatment of human/non-human actors.

→ [Operates On] → ◻ PATCH: A specific interaction, controversy, translation attempt, or mediating artifact within the network-building process.

→ [Produces] → ◻ INVARIANT: A description of the network being assembled/disassembled; identification of key actors, mediators, obligatory passage points, and processes of translation.

→ [Requires] → ◻ CONTRACTION: Focusing strictly on observable associations and transformations, resisting premature explanations based on macro-social categories. "Description is explanation."

→ [Constructs] → ◻ REPRESENTATION: Flat, relational descriptions of actor-networks; critique of traditional sociology's reliance on concepts like "society" or "structure."

→ [Optimizes] → ◻ RECURSION: How successful translations and enrollments stabilize parts of the network, creating black-boxed actors or obligatory passage points that then shape subsequent network formations.

→ [Embeds] → ◻ MODEL: Semiotics (Greimas), sociology of science/technology (Callon, Law), ethnomethodology, philosophy of technology, critique of sociological dualisms.

→ [Projects] → ◻ METRIC: Network extension, stability, durability of inscriptions, success of translations, degree of actor enrollment (descriptive analysis of network dynamics).

→ [Seeks] → ◻ FIXED POINT: A rich, empirical description of how a specific collective or state of affairs was assembled (or failed to be assembled) through the work of heterogeneous actors.

METAMODEL

Distinctions & Crossings:

◻ MODEL (The ANT description/map of the network) | ◻ REALITY (The actors' own complex, ongoing efforts and interpretations) → ◻ MEASUREMENT ARTIFACT (The analyst's description is itself a translation/inscription; controversies arise when actors dispute the analyst's or other actors' descriptions).

◻ TIME-LIKE (The dynamic process of network assembly/disassembly over time) | ◻ SPACE-LIKE (The topology of the assembled network at a given moment) → ◻ CAUSAL CONE (How specific connections and translations (space-like) enable or constrain future network developments over time). ANT emphasizes tracing the time-like process.

◻ CONTINUOUS (Flow of interactions, ambiguity of relations) | ◻ DISCRETE (Identified actors, specific links, successful translations, durable inscriptions) → ◻ SAMPLING THRESHOLD (The ANT analyst's focus on moments of stabilization, controversy, or explicit articulation that make the network-building process observable and describable).

Operations: [Observe] actors (human/non-human) interacting, forming alliances, generating inscriptions. [Encode] observations in terms of translations, enrollments, network links, mediators. [Convolve] an actor's move (PATCH) with the existing network state (KERNEL) to understand its effect on associations. [Entangle] humans, non-humans, texts, technologies within the same analytical plane. [Forget] a priori social explanations; distinctions between micro/macro, social/technical, human/non-human (as explanatory categories).
