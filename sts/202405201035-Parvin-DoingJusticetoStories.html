Focused Problem Statement:

Digital storytelling projects, especially those involving vulnerable individuals, often focus on the technical production or potential positive impact of sharing stories (Level 1 Tool/Output; Level 3 Empowerment Framing). This can obscure the complex ethical responsibilities and power dynamics inherent in mediating and circulating personal narratives (Level 0 Lived Experience). Without careful attention to "doing justice" – considering consent contextually, representational fairness, ownership, potential harm from circulation, and storyteller agency (Level 2 Relations neglected) – these projects risk exploiting participants or causing unintended harm, hidden by a focus on technology or simplistic notions of 'giving voice' (Level 4 Logic: Obscuring Power Dynamics).

1. Core Model and Index

Parvin, N. (2018). Doing justice to stories: On ethics and politics of digital storytelling. Engaging Science, Technology, and Society, 4, 515–534.

MODEL: Explores the ethical and political dimensions of digital storytelling, particularly when involving vulnerable or marginalized communities, arguing for practices that "do justice" to stories by considering issues of representation, consent, ownership, context, and the potential impact of circulation.

METAMODEL

Distinctions & Crossings:

◻ MODEL (The final digital story artifact / The mediator's intended narrative) | ◻ REALITY (The storyteller's complex lived experience, context, and intentions) → ◻ MEASUREMENT ARTIFACT (The digital story itself, assessed for how well it does justice to the reality; potential misrepresentations or harms resulting from the mediation process).

◻ TIME-LIKE (The storyteller's life history; the process of story creation and circulation over time) | ◻ SPACE-LIKE (The structure of the digital narrative; the platform of dissemination) → ◻ CAUSAL CONE (How choices made in constructing the digital story (space-like) can impact the storyteller's life and reputation over time).

◻ CONTINUOUS (Fluidity of lived experience and identity) | ◻ DISCRETE (The edited, framed, bounded digital story; specific consent agreements) → ◻ SAMPLING THRESHOLD (The editorial and consent processes that select, shape, and authorize specific parts of a continuous life story for discrete digital representation).

Operations: [Observe] digital storytelling projects, platforms, and their impacts. [Encode] observations using ethical frameworks focused on justice, power, and representation. [Convolve] specific storytelling choices (PATCH) with principles of narrative justice (KERNEL) to evaluate ethical dimensions. [Entangle] technology, narrative, ethics, power relations, and individual well-being. [Forget] viewing digital storytelling as solely a technical or aesthetic practice devoid of ethical weight.
