Reference: Stengers, Isabelle - Power and Invention (1997)

Focused Problem Statement:

Modern science often operates with a claim to universal validity and authority (Level 3 Framing), using its methods and criteria (Level 1 Scientific Model) to judge and frequently disqualify or "capture" other forms of knowledge-making (e.g., clinical, artisanal, activist - Level 0 Diverse Realities/Practices). This exercise of epistemic power obscures the specific, situated requirements and potential validity of these other practices (Level 2 Practice Ignored). It hinders genuine invention, which Stengers argues often arises from risky, respectful encounters ("diplomacy") within an "ecology of practices" where different modes of knowing can challenge and enrich each other without premature disqualification based on a single dominant logic (Level 4 Logic: Scientific Hegemony).

1. Core Model and Index

Stengers, I. (1997). Power and invention. University of Minnesota Press.

MODEL: Critiques the power dynamics inherent in modern science that often stifle invention and alternative ways of knowing; advocates for an "ecology of practices" where different knowledge forms can coexist and interact creatively, emphasizing slowing down scientific processes to allow for more careful, situated, and inventive thought.

Index:

◻ Modern Science → (often exercises) → ◻ Power / Exclusion

◻ Invention / Creativity → (requires challenging) → ◻ Dominant Scientific Norms

◻ Ecology of Practices → (allows for coexistence of) → ◻ Diverse Knowledges

◻ Slow Science → (fosters) → ◻ Careful / Situated / Inventive Thought

2. Four-Level Analysis

Illustrative Olog Analysis (Scientific Study Evaluating Acupuncture):

  • Level 0: Base World

* <Reality> = <Patient's embodied experience, Complex physiological interactions, Therapeutic relationship, Phenomena potentially addressed by acupuncture>

  • Level 1: Machine Layer

* <Randomized Controlled Trial (RCT) Protocol (Scientific Model)> [simulates] → <Model requiring double-blinding, placebo control, standardized intervention, statistical significance> (Problem: Model may be ill-suited to acupuncture's specific requirements).

* <RCT> [outputs] → <Statistical results often showing 'no significant effect' beyond placebo>

* <RCT Requirements (Blinding, Standardization) [filter] → <Practice of acupuncture, potentially removing key elements like practitioner skill, rapport, individualized treatment> (Problem: Filtering mechanism destroys the phenomenon under study).

  • Level 2: Human Use Layer

* <Scientific Researchers> [steer/interpret] → <RCT according to established scientific validity criteria> (Problem: Interpreting null result as disproof, not methodological mismatch).

* <Acupuncturists/Patients> [experience/practice] → <A different modality whose requirements are violated by the RCT>

* <Scientific Community/Regulators> [curate] → <Acceptance/Rejection of acupuncture based on RCT results>

  • Level 3: Reflexive Framing Layer

* <Stengers (Theorist)> [models] → <Encounter as potential "capture" by scientific practice's requirements> / [questions] → <Science's claim to universal judgment>

* <Evidence-Based Medicine Framing (RCT focus)> [models] → <RCT as gold standard for all therapeutic claims>

  • Level 4: Latent & Systemic Evaluation

* <Scientific Hegemony (Logic)> [obscures] → <Validity/requirements of other practices; power dynamics in knowledge evaluation> (Problem: Dominant practice imposes its logic, masking others).

* <Universal Method Assumption (Disposition)> [naturalizes] → <Applying one set of criteria (RCT) to all knowledge domains>

* <Ecology of Practices Framework> [reframes] → <Knowledge evaluation as needing attention to specific practice requirements & "diplomacy"> / [elevates] → <Potential for invention through respectful inter-practice encounters>

3. Spatiotemporal Framework

SPATIOTEMPORAL FRAMEWORK

◻ SPATIOTEMPORAL UNIVERSE: Encounters between different knowledge-making practices, particularly modern science and other modes of knowing/doing (clinical, artisanal, political).

→ [Contains] → ◻ FIELD: The dynamic interactions, conflicts, collaborations, and power relations between diverse "practices" defined by their specific requirements, constraints, and criteria for success.

→ [Has] → ◻ DOMAIN: Different knowledge practices (scientific labs, clinical settings, activist groups) × Their specific material setups & tools × Their characteristic questions & criteria × Power dynamics between practices × Time (unfolding of encounters).

→ [Projects] → ◻ SIGNAL: Scientific papers, clinical protocols, activist strategies, expert testimonies, accounts of collaboration or conflict, moments of invention or failure resulting from interactions, discourses of validation/disqualification. (ℝᵏ representing practice-specific criteria, power indicators, outcomes of interaction).

→ [Admits] → ◻ PERTURBATION: Establishment of collaborations, imposition of one practice's criteria on another, moments of mutual learning or invention, breakdown of communication, political interventions shaping interactions.

→ [Observed Through] → ◻ WINDOW: Pragmatist Philosophy of Science (influenced by Whitehead, James, Deleuze), STS, focus on the "ecology" and specific requirements of practices.

→ [Defines] → ◻ OBSERVATION SCALE: Meso-level analysis of the interactions and power dynamics between distinct knowledge practices.

→ [Delimits] → ◻ CONTEXT BOUNDARY: Rejects judging all practices by a single (usually scientific) standard; focuses on the specific conditions of existence and success internal to each practice and the risks/potentials of their encounters.

→ [Generates] → ◻ SAMPLING LATTICE: Analysis of historical or contemporary cases involving interactions between science and other fields (e.g., hypnosis research, patient activism, technological innovation outside labs).

→ [Manifests As] → ◻ PHENOMENON: Knowledge practices as distinct "ecologies" with specific requirements ("obligations"); modern science often exercises power by "capturing" or disqualifying other practices; genuine invention often arises from risky, respectful encounters where practices impose their requirements on each other, requiring "diplomacy" and "slowing down."

◻ ENGINE: Ecological / Pragmatist Analysis of Interacting Practices.

→ [Applies] → ◻ TRANSFORM: Analyzing encounters between practices not in terms of universal truth, but in terms of power, mutual interference, specific requirements, and the potential for pragmatic invention ("what does this practice make possible?").

→ [Operates On] → ◻ PATCH: A specific interaction, conflict, collaboration, or moment of invention involving different knowledge practices (e.g., a clinical trial involving patient input, a dispute over expert authority).

→ [Produces] → ◻ INVARIANT: Identification of the specific requirements and constraints of the involved practices; analysis of the power dynamics (capture vs. diplomacy); assessment of inventive potential.

→ [Requires] → ◻ CONTRACTION: Focusing on the specific conditions and criteria that give each practice its coherence and efficacy, rather than applying external judgments.

→ [Constructs] → ◻ REPRESENTATION: The concept of an "ecology of practices"; critique of scientific hegemonism ("capture"); model for fostering inventive collaborations ("diplomacy," "slow science").

→ [Optimizes] → ◻ RECURSION: How respectful encounters, where practices articulate their requirements, can lead to mutual transformation and novel inventions that wouldn't arise within a single practice alone.

→ [Embeds] → ◻ MODEL: Process philosophy (Whitehead), pragmatism (James), Deleuze & Guattari, STS (Latour, Callon influences), feminist philosophy of science.

→ [Projects] → ◻ METRIC: Degree of mutual respect/understanding between practices, identification of power imbalances ("capture"), potential for genuine invention, ethical implications of interactions (qualitative assessment).

→ [Seeks] → ◻ FIXED POINT: Fostering conditions for an "ecology of practices" where diverse forms of knowledge can thrive and inventively interact without destructive power plays.

METAMODEL

Distinctions & Crossings:

◻ MODEL (One practice's attempt to impose its model/criteria universally, e.g., scientific reductionism) | ◻ REALITY (The diverse ecology of practices, each with its own valid requirements and modes of success) → ◻ MEASUREMENT ARTIFACT (Instances of "capture" where one practice overrides another; or, conversely, moments of "diplomacy" and invention resulting from respectful engagement).

◻ TIME-LIKE (The unfolding process of interaction and invention/capture over time) | ◻ SPACE-LIKE (The "ecology" – the landscape of coexisting practices and their power relations) → ◻ CAUSAL CONE (How the existing power relations within the ecology (space-like) shape the trajectory and potential outcomes of encounters between practices over time).

◻ CONTINUOUS (The potential fluidity and overlap between practices) | ◻ DISCRETE (Analytically distinct practices defined by their specific requirements, obligations, and criteria) → ◻ SAMPLING THRESHOLD (The pragmatist analysis identifying the specific demands and constraints that differentiate one practice from another within the ecology).

Operations: [Observe] interactions between different knowledge-making communities/practices. [Encode] observations using concepts of ecology, requirements, obligations, capture, diplomacy. [Convolve] the actions of one practice (PATCH) with the requirements of another (KERNEL) during an encounter to analyze power/invention. [Entangle] knowledge, power, ethics, practice, invention. [Forget] assuming a single hierarchy of knowledge or a universal scientific method applicable to all domains.
