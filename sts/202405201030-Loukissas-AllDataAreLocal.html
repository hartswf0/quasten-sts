Focused Problem Statement:

Data analysis and visualization practices frequently treat datasets as abstract, placeless resources representing objective reality (Level 0 assumed). This framing (Level 3) obscures the crucial fact that all data are local: products of specific instruments, classification choices, labor, representational formats, and socio-material contexts ("data settings" - Level 1 Filters/Simulation). Ignoring data's locality during interpretation and use (Level 2) can lead to serious misinterpretations, spurious claims of universality, and flawed decisions, because the systemic tendency (Level 4 Logic) is to value abstracted data over situated understanding.

1. Core Model and Index

Loukissas, Y. (2019). All data are local: Thinking critically in a data-driven society. MIT Press.

MODEL: Argues against the perception of data as abstract, universal, or objective, emphasizing that all data are "local"—derived from specific contexts, instruments, practices, and interpretations; advocates for critical engagement with data that acknowledges its origins, limitations, and situatedness.

2. Four-Level Analysis

Illustrative Olog Analysis (Using Historical Census Data for Digital Humanities):

  • Level 0: Base World

* <Reality> = <Complex social realities, identities, household structures of past populations>

  • Level 1: Machine Layer

* <Digitized Historical Census Dataset> [simulates] → <Model representing past population via specific categories, definitions, collection methods used *at that time*> (Problem: Model reflects historical locality, not just Level 0 reality).

* <Dataset/Database> [outputs] → <Structured data tables amenable to computational analysis>

* <Census Categories/Collection Practices (Local Filters)> [filter] → <How individuals/households were classified (e.g., limited race/gender categories, enumerator biases)> (Problem: Locality of original filtering is obscured in digital format).

  • Level 2: Human Use Layer

* <Digital Humanities Researcher (User)> [interprets/analyzes] → <Data using computational tools, potentially assuming categories map directly to current understandings or objective reality> (Problem: Use ignores data's local provenance).

* <Archivists/Digitizers> [steer/curate] → <Selection, digitization format, metadata creation (introducing further locality)>

  • Level 3: Reflexive Framing Layer

* <Loukissas (Theorist)> [models] → <Data as Local Artifact of specific "Data Setting"> / [questions] → <View of data as abstract/universal resource>

* <Big Data/Computational Framing> [models] → <Data as raw material for pattern discovery, often downplaying context>

  • Level 4: Latent & Systemic Evaluation

* <Data Abstractness Myth (Logic)> [obscures] → <Material origins, embedded assumptions, limitations of data> (Problem: Core misunderstanding of data's nature).

* <Valuing Scale over Context (Disposition)> [naturalizes] → <Analysis detached from provenance>

* <"All Data Are Local" Framework> [reframes] → <Data interpretation as requiring critical attention to origin/context> / [elevates] → <Data provenance, metadata, situated analysis>

SPATIOTEMPORAL FRAMEWORK

◻ SPATIOTEMPORAL UNIVERSE: Contemporary data-driven society; specific sites of data collection, processing, and interpretation.

→ [Contains] → ◻ FIELD: Practices of data creation, analysis, visualization, and use across various domains (urban planning, journalism, science, humanities).

→ [Has] → ◻ DOMAIN: Data sources (sensors, archives, surveys) × Tools/Algorithms for processing × Representational formats (databases, visualizations) × Local contexts of creation/interpretation × Time (data lifecycle).

→ [Projects] → ◻ SIGNAL: Datasets, databases, data visualizations, dashboards, algorithms processing data, interpretations/narratives based on data, metadata. (ℝᵏ representing data values, statistical summaries, visualization parameters).

→ [Admits] → ◻ PERTURBATION: Changes in data collection methods, updates to datasets, new visualization techniques, critiques of data quality/bias, alternative interpretations, data decay/loss.

→ [Observed Through] → ◻ WINDOW: Critical Data Studies, STS, ethnography of data practices, focus on data provenance and situatedness.

→ [Defines] → ◻ OBSERVATION SCALE: Focus on the specific, material, situated practices involved in making and using data in particular settings ("data setting").

→ [Delimits] → ◻ CONTEXT BOUNDARY: Rejects the view of data as abstract, objective, or placeless; emphasizes the local circumstances of its origin, processing, and interpretation.

→ [Generates] → ◻ SAMPLING LATTICE: Case studies examining datasets related to urban trees, flight paths, historical photographs, ecological monitoring, analyzing their origins, formats, and interpretations within specific communities/projects.

→ [Manifests As] → ◻ PHENOMENON: "All Data Are Local" – Data are not raw or universal but are products of specific local conditions, instruments, classification choices, labor, institutional contexts, and representational formats; their meaning is derived from understanding this locality.

◻ ENGINE: Critical Analysis of Data Setting / Provenance.

→ [Applies] → ◻ TRANSFORM: Shifting focus from data as abstract representation to data as material artifact emerging from a specific "data setting"; emphasizing provenance, context, and materiality.

→ [Operates On] → ◻ PATCH: A specific dataset, visualization, or data-driven claim (e.g., a map of city trees, airline on-time statistics, a digital humanities text analysis).

→ [Produces] → ◻ INVARIANT: Identification of the local specificities (instruments, classifications, labor, context) shaping the data; critique of claims based on decontextualized data.

→ [Requires] → ◻ CONTRACTION: Focusing deeply on the details of data's origin, processing history, and intended use context, rather than its surface appearance or aggregate statistics.

→ [Constructs] → ◻ REPRESENTATION: A model of data as locally situated artifact; concepts like "data setting"; methods for critically interrogating data provenance and context.

→ [Optimizes] → ◻ RECURSION: How the way data is collected and formatted (reflecting local choices) shapes the kinds of analysis and interpretation that can be done later, often reinforcing the initial local perspective.

→ [Embeds] → ◻ MODEL: STS (esp. infrastructure studies, sociology of knowledge), critical cartography/GIS, archival studies, media theory (materiality).

→ [Projects] → ◻ METRIC: Richness of contextual understanding, awareness of data limitations/biases, potential for misinterpretation due to locality neglect (qualitative critical assessment).

→ [Seeks] → ◻ FIXED POINT: A situated, critical understanding of specific datasets that acknowledges their locality and avoids spurious claims of universality or objectivity.

METAMODEL

Distinctions & Crossings:

◻ MODEL (The dataset/visualization presented as objective or universal) | ◻ REALITY (The complex, messy local circumstances of the data's creation and the phenomenon it purports to represent) → ◻ MEASUREMENT ARTIFACT (The data itself, understood as an artifact shaped by local choices, instruments, classifications, and contexts).

◻ TIME-LIKE (The lifecycle of data from collection to analysis to interpretation; historical context) | ◻ SPACE-LIKE (The specific location, instruments, institutional setting of data creation - the "data setting") → ◻ CAUSAL CONE (How the specific conditions of the data setting (space-like) shape the characteristics and potential interpretations of the data over its lifecycle (time-like)). Locality (space-like) is foregrounded.

◻ CONTINUOUS (The richness of the real-world phenomenon) | ◻ DISCRETE (The specific variables measured, categories used, data points collected, pixels in visualization) → ◻ SAMPLING THRESHOLD (The local choices about what aspects of reality to capture, how to measure them, and how to format them, imposing discrete structure).

Operations: [Observe] how specific datasets are created, processed, visualized, interpreted. [Encode] findings by emphasizing the "data setting" – instruments, classifications, labor, context. [Convolve] data representations (PATCH) with their local provenance (KERNEL) to critically assess their meaning/limitations. [Entangle] data with its material origins, social contexts, and representational forms. [Forget] treating data as raw, objective, or self-evident input.
