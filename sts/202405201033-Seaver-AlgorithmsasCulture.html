Focused Problem Statement:

Analyses of algorithmic systems (e.g., music recommendation - Level 1 Machine) often treat them solely as technical objects performing objective functions (calculating similarity, predicting preference - Level 3 Technical Framing). This obscures the crucial way algorithms function *as culture*: they are embedded within and shape webs of human meaning-making, interpretation, and practice (Level 0 Reality). Both developers and users create "folk theories" to understand and interact with these systems (Level 2 Use/Interpretation), and the algorithms themselves embody cultural assumptions. Focusing only on technical function ignores this vital cultural dimension (Level 4 Obscuring) and hinders a full understanding of how algorithms operate in the world.

1. Core Model and Index

Seaver, N. (2017). Algorithms as culture: Some tactics for the ethnography of algorithmic systems. Big Data & Society, 4(2).

MODEL: Proposes conceptualizing algorithms not just as technical objects but as "culture," arguing for ethnographic approaches to study them; suggests tactics like "studying sideways" (examining adjacent practices) and "following the engineers" to understand how algorithms are made meaningful and embedded in social life.

SPATIOTEMPORAL FRAMEWORK

◻ SPATIOTEMPORAL UNIVERSE: Contemporary settings where algorithms shape experiences and decisions (e.g., music recommendation, content filtering).

→ [Contains] → ◻ FIELD: The socio-technical systems surrounding algorithms, including their design, implementation, interpretation, and the practices they mediate.

→ [Has] → ◻ DOMAIN: Algorithmic code × Data inputs × Human developers/users × Organizational contexts × Cultural interpretations/meanings × Time (algorithm development/use/evolution).

→ [Projects] → ◻ SIGNAL: Algorithmic outputs (recommendations, classifications), developer discourse ("folk theories"), user interpretations and practices, interface designs mediating algorithms, documentation, traces of algorithmic behavior. (ℝᵏ representing output patterns, user behavior metrics, discourse themes).

→ [Admits] → ◻ PERTURBATION: Algorithm updates, changes in data, user adaptation/resistance, shifts in developer understanding, public controversy about algorithmic effects.

→ [Observed Through] → ◻ WINDOW: Ethnography of algorithmic systems; conceptualizing algorithms as culture (shared meanings, practices, interpretations); STS.

→ [Defines] → ◻ OBSERVATION SCALE: Meso-level focus on the local cultures surrounding specific algorithms – how developers, users, and others make sense of and interact with them.

→ [Delimits] → ◻ CONTEXT BOUNDARY: Moves beyond viewing algorithms solely as technical objects to seeing them as embedded in and shaping cultural practices and understandings.

→ [Generates] → ◻ SAMPLING LATTICE: Ethnographic fieldwork with algorithm developers (e.g., music recommendation engineers), users, analysis of related discourse; tactics like "studying sideways" (related practices) and "following engineers."

→ [Manifests As] → ◻ PHENOMENON: Algorithms functioning as culture – not just technical tools, but sites where meaning is made, contested, and negotiated by developers and users who develop "folk theories" and practices around them; algorithms embody cultural assumptions and shape cultural experiences.

◻ ENGINE: Ethnographic Analysis of Algorithmic Cultures.

→ [Applies] → ◻ TRANSFORM: Treating algorithms and their surrounding practices as a cultural system amenable to ethnographic interpretation, focusing on meaning-making.

→ [Operates On] → ◻ PATCH: Specific instances of algorithm design discussions, user interactions with recommendations, developer explanations ("folk theories"), interface elements.

→ [Produces] → ◻ INVARIANT: Identification of the cultural logics, shared understandings, and interpretive practices surrounding a specific algorithmic system among its developers and/or users.

→ [Requires] → ◻ CONTRACTION: Focusing on the interpretive and meaning-making aspects of human engagement with algorithms, rather than just their technical function or quantitative output.

→ [Constructs] → ◻ REPRESENTATION: Ethnographic accounts of algorithmic cultures; the theoretical argument for understanding algorithms as culture; methodological tactics for ethnographic study.

→ [Optimizes] → ◻ RECURSION: How the cultural understandings (folk theories) held by developers shape algorithm design, which in turn shapes user experience and interpretation, potentially reinforcing or modifying those cultural understandings.

→ [Embeds] → ◻ MODEL: Cultural anthropology (ethnography, culture concept), STS (actor-network theory, social construction), symbolic interactionism, media studies.

→ [Projects] → ◻ METRIC: Richness of ethnographic description, coherence of identified cultural logics/folk theories, understanding of situated meaning-making around algorithms (qualitative).

→ [Seeks] → ◻ FIXED POINT: A deep ethnographic understanding of how a specific algorithmic system is culturally embedded and made meaningful by those who build and interact with it.

METAMODEL

Distinctions & Crossings:

◻ MODEL (Developer/user "folk theories" about the algorithm / Ethnographer's model of the algorithmic culture) | ◻ REALITY (The complex, often opaque functioning of the algorithm and its entanglement with diverse practices) → ◻ MEASUREMENT ARTIFACT (Observed practices, expressed beliefs, interface designs that reflect attempts to make sense of or interact with the algorithm; the ethnography itself).

◻ TIME-LIKE (Evolution of the algorithm and the culture around it) | ◻ SPACE-LIKE (The structure of the algorithm, the organization of the development team, the user community) → ◻ CAUSAL CONE (How the existing technical and social structures (space-like) shape the ongoing development and interpretation of the algorithm over time).

◻ CONTINUOUS (Flow of data, subtle algorithmic adjustments, fluid user interpretations) | ◻ DISCRETE (Specific recommendations, defined folk theories, distinct user practices, analytical categories of the ethnographer) → ◻ SAMPLING THRESHOLD (The points where developers, users, or ethnographers make sense of the continuous algorithmic process by identifying discrete patterns, rules, or meanings).

Operations: [Observe] developers, users, and discourse around specific algorithms via ethnography. [Encode] observations in terms of cultural practices, folk theories, meaning-making. [Convolve] specific interactions (PATCH) with the identified algorithmic culture (KERNEL) to interpret their significance. [Entangle] technical code, human interpretation, and cultural context. [Forget] viewing algorithms as purely technical, objective, or self-contained systems devoid of cultural meaning.
