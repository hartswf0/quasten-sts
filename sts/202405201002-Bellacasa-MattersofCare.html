Focused Problem Statement:

Dominant techno-scientific and socio-economic frameworks often model the world (Level 1 Models) in terms of discrete, productive entities and instrumental relations, rendering invisible or devaluing the essential, ongoing relational work of "care" – including maintenance, repair, attunement, and affective labor – required to sustain complex socio-ecological systems (Level 0 Reality). This systematic obscuring (Level 4 Logic) of "matters of care" hides the dependencies, vulnerabilities, and ethical obligations within human and more-than-human entanglements (Level 2 Experience/Practice neglected), limiting our capacity for speculative thinking (Level 3 Reframing needed) about more just and sustainable ways of living together.

1. Core Model and Index

Bellacasa, M. P. de la. (2017). Matters of care. University of Minnesota Press.

MODEL: Rearticulates "care" as a vital ethico-political concept for thinking about more-than-human worlds and techno-scientific practices, moving beyond purely human-centric or sentimental notions to emphasize care as a material, practical, and speculative engagement with complex entanglements and obligations.

2. Four-Level Analysis

Illustrative Olog Analysis (Industrial Agriculture vs. Soil Care Practice):

  • Level 0: Base World

* <Reality> = <Complex Soil Ecology (microbes, fungi, nutrients, water cycles, plant health)>

  • Level 1: Machine Layer

* <Industrial Agriculture Model> [simulates] → <Model of soil as inert substrate for chemical inputs maximizing monocrop yield> (Problem: Model ignores Level 0 ecological complexity).

* <Industrial Practices (Tilling, Pesticides)> [outputs] → <High short-term yield, Degraded soil structure, Nutrient runoff>

* <Yield-Maximization Logic> [filters] → <Focus on crop output, ignoring soil health indicators & long-term effects> (Problem: Filtering mechanism devalues care).

  • Level 2: Human Use Layer

* <Industrial Farmer/Agribusiness> [steers] → <Application of inputs based on maximizing yield/profit>

* <Soil Microbes/Ecosystem> [experience] → <Degradation, loss of biodiversity>

* <Soil Care Practitioner (e.g., permaculture)> [interprets/steers] → <Practices based on observing soil health, fostering relationships> (Problem: This practice often devalued/invisible in dominant system).

  • Level 3: Reflexive Framing Layer

* <Bellacasa (Theorist)> [models] → <Soil care as 'Matters of Care' (practical, ethical, political)> / [questions] → <Instrumental views of nature>

* <Dominant Economic Framing> [models] → <Soil/Care as externalities or low-value inputs>

  • Level 4: Latent & Systemic Evaluation

* <Productivist Logic (Framework Logic)> [obscures] → <Value of maintenance, relational work, ecological health> (Problem: Systemic logic inherently undervalues care).

* <Human Exceptionalism (Disposition)> [naturalizes] → <Exploitation of 'natural resources'>

* <Matters of Care Framework> [reframes] → <Sustainability as requiring ongoing relational care practices> / [elevates] → <Interdependence and response-ability>

SPATIOTEMPORAL FRAMEWORK

◻ SPATIOTEMPORAL UNIVERSE: Contemporary techno-scientific and ecological worlds characterized by complex interdependencies and often hidden labor/relations.

→ [Contains] → ◻ FIELD: Socio-technical and ecological practices, focusing on relations involving knowledge, technology, humans, and non-humans.

→ [Has] → ◻ DOMAIN: Relational networks × Situated practices × Ethical dimensions.

→ [Projects] → ◻ SIGNAL: Practices of maintenance, repair, nurturing, knowledge transmission, affective labor, signs of flourishing or neglect. (ℝᵏ representing indicators of relational health, work invested, dependencies met).

→ [Admits] → ◻ PERTURBATION: Technological change, environmental disruption, shifts in political/economic focus, conscious interventions of care or neglect.

→ [Observed Through] → ◻ WINDOW: Feminist STS, speculative ethics, process philosophy focused on "care."

→ [Defines] → ◻ OBSERVATION SCALE: From micro-interactions (lab work, gardening) to macro-level systems (soil ecologies, knowledge commons).

→ [Delimits] → ◻ CONTEXT BOUNDARY: Expands focus beyond human agency to include non-human needs and contributions; highlights affective and practical dimensions often ignored.

→ [Generates] → ◻ SAMPLING LATTICE: Case studies like soil care, animal experimentation ethics, open source software maintenance, permaculture.

→ [Manifests As] → ◻ PHENOMENON: "Matters of care" – the necessary, often mundane, yet vital ethico-political practices sustaining more-than-human worlds; care as thinking, practice, and obligation.

◻ ENGINE: Ethico-political Re-articulation of Care.

→ [Applies] → ◻ TRANSFORM: Re-framing diverse practices (scientific, technical, agricultural, domestic) through the lens of care's multiple dimensions (labor, affect, ethics, politics).

→ [Operates On] → ◻ PATCH: A specific situation or practice involving interdependence (e.g., maintaining a seed bank, debugging code).

→ [Produces] → ◻ INVARIANT: The underlying necessity and political significance of care work; the entanglement generating obligations.

→ [Requires] → ◻ CONTRACTION: Abstracting the common elements of maintenance, attention, and response-ability across diverse contexts.

→ [Constructs] → ◻ REPRESENTATION: A model of the world as constituted by and dependent on ongoing, situated care relations.

→ [Optimizes] → ◻ RECURSION: Speculative thinking ("what if we cared differently?") to imagine and foster alternative caring futures.

→ [Embeds] → ◻ MODEL: Feminist theories of care ethics, Haraway's companion species, Stengers' ecology of practices, process philosophy.

→ [Projects] → ◻ METRIC: Flourishing, sustainability, recognition of interdependence, ethical response-ability (qualitative).

→ [Seeks] → ◻ FIXED POINT: A state of acknowledged interdependence and active, speculative caring relations (an ongoing process, not a static point).

METAMODEL

Distinctions & Crossings:

◻ MODEL (Theory/Concept of Care) | ◻ REALITY (Actual lived practices/entanglements) → ◻ MEASUREMENT ARTIFACT (The recognition and articulation of specific, often invisible, actions as significant "matters of care").

◻ TIME-LIKE (Ongoing processes of maintenance/relation) | ◻ SPACE-LIKE (Networks of interdependence) → ◻ CAUSAL CONE (Web of obligations and consequences arising from entangled relations).

◻ CONTINUOUS (Flow of relations/needs) | ◻ DISCRETE (Specific acts of care, interventions, moments of decision) → ◻ SAMPLING THRESHOLD (Identifying particular practices or moments as significant instantiations of care/neglect).

Operations: [Observe] practices of connection, maintenance, support. [Encode] them using the multi-dimensional vocabulary of care. [Convolve] specific actions with their relational context to reveal ethical weight. [Entangle] human and non-human well-being, technology and responsibility. [Forget] purely instrumental views of relations or technology.
