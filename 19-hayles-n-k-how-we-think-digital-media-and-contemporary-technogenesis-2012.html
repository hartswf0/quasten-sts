Hayles N K How We Think Digital Media And Contemporary Technogenesis 2012

1. Problem Statement

  1. Hayles, N. K. - How We Think: Digital Media and Contemporary Technogenesis (2012)

Focused Problem Statement: Judging online reading practices (L2) simply as degraded attention compared to print norms (L0 Traditional Cognition) obscures the process of technogenesis modeled by Hayles (L3 Framing). The web interface (L1 Machine Layer) simulates an information space that encourages "hyper attention," a cognitive style co-evolving with the technology, reflecting latent neuroplasticity (L4) rather than mere decline.

2. Core Model

19. Hayles, N. K. - How We Think: Digital Media and Contemporary Technogenesis (2012)

  • MODEL: Argues that human cognition is undergoing a fundamental co-evolution with digital technologies ("technogenesis"). Explores how practices like hyper-reading and database querying, fostered by digital media, are changing cognitive modes, moving from deep attention towards machine-like pattern recognition and data retrieval.
  • Index:
    • ◻ Human Cognition ←→ ◻ Digital Technologies (Co-evolution / Technogenesis)
    • ◻ Digital Media → ◻ Fostering New Cognitive Practices (Hyper-reading, Database Logic)
    • ◻ New Cognitive Practices → ◻ Shift from Deep Attention → ◻ Towards Pattern Recognition/Data Retrieval

3. Four-Level Analysis

19. Hayles, N. K. - How We Think: Digital Media and Contemporary Technogenesis (2012)

System: Reading news online, involving clicking through multiple linked articles and skimming content.

Level 0: <Reality> = <Human Cognitive Architecture (Attention, Memory Systems)> & <Traditional Print Reading Practices>

Level 1: <Machine Layer> = <Web Browser & News Website Interface>
<Web Interface> [simulates] → <Information Space Navigable via Hyperlinks>
<Web Interface> [outputs] → <Fragmented Text Chunks, Embedded Links, Multimedia Stimuli>
<Web Interface> [filters] → <Linear Reading Path> in favor of <User-Driven Exploration & Distraction>

Level 2: <Human Use Layer> = <Online Reader>
<Online Reader> [steers] → <Information Intake> via <Clicking, Scrolling, Skimming ('Hyper Reading')>
<Online Reader> [interprets] → <Information> through <Mode of Attention shaped by Interface (Hyper Attention)>

Level 3: <Reflexive Framing Layer> = <Hayles (Media/Cognition Theorist)>
<Hayles> [models] → <Hyper Reading/Attention> as <Cognitive Adaptation Co-Evolving with Digital Environments (Technogenesis)>
<Hayles> [challenges ontology] → <Of Fixed Human Cognition>, seeing it as <Plastic & Technologically Entangled>
<Hayles> [historicizes] → <Shift from Deep Attention (Print) to Hyper Attention (Digital)>

Level 4: <Latent & Systemic Evaluation> = <Neuroplasticity / Co-Evolutionary Framework / Value Placed on Different Cognitive Modes>
<Co-Evolutionary Framework> [naturalizes] → <Entanglement of Mind & Technology>
<System Logic> [obscures] → <Potential Downsides of Hyper Attention (loss of deep focus) without dismissing its value>
<Cultural Disposition> [reframes] → <Cognitive Changes> not as simple degradation, but as <Adaptive Shift>

4. Spatiotemporal Framework

19. Hayles, N. K. - How We Think: Digital Media and Contemporary Technogenesis. (2012)

  • ◻ SPATIOTEMPORAL UNIVERSE: Contemporary human culture deeply intertwined with digital technologies.
    • → [Contains] → ◻ FIELD: The interaction between human cognitive processes and digital media environments.
      • → [Has] → ◻ DOMAIN: Human brains/bodies, cognitive habits (reading, attention), digital devices, software interfaces, databases × Current techno-cultural era.
      • → [Projects] → ◻ SIGNAL: Reading patterns (deep vs. hyper), attention spans, information seeking behaviors, neural activity patterns (potentially), discourse about digital effects on thinking. (ℝᵏ representing reading speed, link-clicking frequency, task-switching rates).
      • → [Admits] → ◻ PERTURBATION: New digital tools/interfaces, educational practices adapting to digital media, conscious efforts to cultivate different attention modes.
    • → [Observed Through] → ◻ WINDOW: Cognitive science, media studies, literary studies (digital humanities), theories of embodiment and distributed cognition (Technogenesis).
      • → [Defines] → ◻ OBSERVATION SCALE: Focus on the co-evolution of human cognitive styles and technological environments, bridging neuroscience, humanities, and media analysis.
      • → [Delimits] → ◻ CONTEXT BOUNDARY: Examines the shift from deep attention associated with print to hyper attention associated with digital media, seeing them as complementary rather than simply oppositional.
      • → [Generates] → ◻ SAMPLING LATTICE: Analysis of reading practices (print vs. screen), digital interfaces, database structures, cognitive studies on attention, examples from digital literature/art.
    • → [Manifests As] → ◻ PHENOMENON: Technogenesis & Shifting Cognitive Modes – Human cognition is dynamically co-evolving with its technological environment. Digital media foster and reward "hyper attention" (scanning, multitasking, seeking stimuli) alongside the traditional "deep attention" (sustained focus, concentration) associated with print, leading to a transformation in how "we" think.
  • ◻ ENGINE: Comparative Media Cognition Analysis / Technogenetic Synthesis.
    • → [Applies] → ◻ TRANSFORM: Comparing cognitive affordances and effects of different media regimes (print vs. digital), analyzing feedback loops between technology use and cognitive habits.
      • → [Operates On] → ◻ PATCH: Specific reading practices, interface designs, cognitive tasks performed in different media environments.
      • → [Produces] → ◻ INVARIANT: The concepts of hyper/deep attention as technologically correlated cognitive modes; the principle of technogenesis (co-evolution).
      • → [Requires] → ◻ CONTRACTION: Bridging insights from disparate fields (neuroscience, humanities, media studies) to identify patterns of cognitive-technological interaction.
    • → [Constructs] → ◻ REPRESENTATION: A model of contemporary cognition as dynamically shaped by, and shaping, digital technologies.
    • → [Optimizes] → ◻ RECURSION: Examining technogenesis across different cognitive domains (reading, memory, attention) and technological forms.
      • → [Embeds] → ◻ MODEL: Theories of neuroplasticity, embodied/extended/distributed cognition, media theory (McLuhan influence), literary analysis.
      • → [Projects] → ◻ METRIC: Prevalence of hyper/deep attention modes, adaptive cognitive strategies, changes in information processing skills (qualitative/descriptive).
      • → [Seeks] → ◻ FIXED POINT: Understanding the ongoing co-evolutionary trajectory of human cognition and digital technology.
  • ◻ METAMODEL: Theory of Technogenesis / Comparative Media Cognition. Governs the analysis of mind-technology interactions. Binds Technological Environments/Affordances DOMAIN ↔ Human Cognitive Styles/Processes CODMAIN.
  • Distinctions & Crossings:
    • ◻ MODEL (Cognitive science models/Hayles' theory) | ◻ REALITY (Complex, ongoing neurological/cognitive processes) → ◻ MEASUREMENT ARTIFACT (Current cognitive measures are artifacts influenced by the very technologies being studied). Hayles emphasizes the model participates in reality (technogenesis).
    • ◻ TIME-LIKE (Co-evolution of cognition and technology over time) | ◻ SPACE-LIKE (Neural structures, digital architectures). Focuses on how interactions in digital SPACE change cognition over TIME.
    • ◻ CONTINUOUS (Cognitive processes, technological development) | ◻ DISCRETE (Hyper vs. Deep attention modes, specific technologies) → ◻ SAMPLING THRESHOLD (Uses discrete categories/examples to analyze the continuous co-evolution).
  • Operations: Observe cognitive behaviors in relation to media use. Encode these behaviors using categories like hyper/deep attention. Convolve cognitive tendencies (KERNEL) with media environments (PATCH) showing mutual shaping. Entangle neurological processes (FEATURE) with technological use (FEATURE). Tries not to Forget the embodied and distributed nature of cognition.
