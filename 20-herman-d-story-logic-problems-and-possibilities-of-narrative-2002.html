Herman D Story Logic Problems And Possibilities Of Narrative 2002

1. Problem Statement

  1. Herman, D. - Story Logic: Problems and Possibilities of Narrative (2002)

Focused Problem Statement: Analyzing complex narratives like Faulkner's (L1 Discursive Structure) solely based on textual features obscures the active cognitive work performed by the reader (L2 Human Use). Herman's "Story Logic" (L3 Framing) models comprehension as the reader's simulation of a storyworld (L0 Reality) using cognitive schemas to make inferences from discursive cues, revealing mental model building as central, often underemphasized by purely structuralist accounts (L4 Competing Paradigm).

2. Core Model

20. Herman, D. - Story Logic: Problems and Possibilities of Narrative (2002)

  • MODEL: Develops a cognitive approach to narrative theory, arguing that storytelling and understanding stories ("story logic") are fundamental human cognitive processes. Analyzes the underlying structures, mental models, and interpretive strategies readers/listeners use to make sense of narratives across various media.
  • Index:
    • ◻ Narrative (Storytelling/Understanding) → ◻ Fundamental Human Cognitive Process (Story Logic)
    • ◻ Story Logic → ◻ Use of Cognitive Structures/Mental Models/Interpretive Strategies
    • ◻ Narrative Analysis → ◻ Focus on Cognitive Processes across Media

3. Four-Level Analysis

20. Herman, D. - Story Logic: Problems and Possibilities of Narrative (2002)

System: Understanding the plot of a complex novel with multiple timelines (e.g., Faulkner's "The Sound and the Fury").

Level 0: <Reality> = <Sequence of Events in the Fictional Storyworld> & <Basic Human Cognitive Capacities (Memory, Inference)>

Level 1: <Machine Layer> = <The Novel's Discursive Structure (Non-linear narration, focalization shifts)>
<Novel's Structure> [simulates] → <Character Consciousness / Fragmented Experience> rather than <Linear Chronology>
<Novel's Structure> [outputs] → <Narrative Text Presenting Events Out of Order>
<Novel's Structure> [filters] → <Direct Access to Chronological Fabula> demanding <Inferential Work from Reader>

Level 2: <Human Use Layer> = <Reader>
<Reader> [steers] → <Mental Model Construction> by <Actively Integrating Cues, Making Inferences, Revising Hypotheses>
<Reader> [interprets] → <Fragmented Discourse> using <Cognitive Schemas & Narrative Conventions ('Story Logic')> to build <Coherent Storyworld Representation>

Level 3: <Reflexive Framing Layer> = <Herman (Cognitive Narratologist)>
<Herman> [models] → <Narrative Comprehension> as <Active Cognitive Process of Mental Model Building>
<Herman> [challenges ontology] → <Of Narrative as solely Textual Structure>, emphasizing <Cognitive Interaction>
<Herman> [elevates] → <Cognitive Science Principles> for <Explaining Narrative Sense-Making>

Level 4: <Latent & Systemic Evaluation> = <Cognitive Science Paradigm / Universalist Assumptions about Cognition>
<Cognitive Paradigm> [naturalizes] → <Mental Models & Schemas as Basis of Understanding>
<Framework Logic> [obscures] → <Potential Cultural Variation in Story Logic or Cognitive Styles> (though adaptable)
<System Logic> [reframes] → <Reading> as <Cognitive Problem-Solving & Simulation>

4. Spatiotemporal Framework

20. Herman, D. - Story Logic: Problems and Possibilities of Narrative (2002)

  • ◻ SPATIOTEMPORAL UNIVERSE: Human experience and communication across various media.
    • → [Contains] → ◻ FIELD: Narrative structures and the cognitive processes involved in producing and understanding stories.
      • → [Has] → ◻ DOMAIN: Narratives (literary, oral, visual, interactive), cognitive schemas, mental models, linguistic cues × Contexts of storytelling/reception.
      • → [Projects] → ◻ SIGNAL: Story elements (plot, character, setting, focalization), discourse structures, cues triggering inferences, readers'/viewers' interpretations. (ℝᵏ representing narrative complexity, inferential load, character depth cues).
      • → [Admits] → ◻ PERTURBATION: Different narrative media (novel vs. film vs. game), unconventional narrative structures, varying reader expertise/background knowledge.
    • → [Observed Through] → ◻ WINDOW: Cognitive science (mental models, schema theory), narratology (structuralist and post-classical), linguistics, artificial intelligence (story understanding).
      • → [Defines] → ◻ OBSERVATION SCALE: Focus on the underlying cognitive mechanisms ("story logic") that enable narrative comprehension and creation across media.
      • → [Delimits] → ◻ CONTEXT BOUNDARY: Privileges cognitive processing, seeks general principles of narrative sense-making, integrates insights from diverse fields.
      • → [Generates] → ◻ SAMPLING LATTICE: Analysis of specific narrative passages/sequences, reader response data (experimental or inferred), computational models of story understanding, cross-media comparisons.
    • → [Manifests As] → ◻ PHENOMENON: Narrative as Cognitive Sense-Making – Storytelling and story comprehension are fundamental human cognitive activities based on constructing mental models ("storyworlds") using cues from the narrative discourse and drawing on existing knowledge structures (schemas, scripts) to make inferences and achieve coherence.
  • ◻ ENGINE: Cognitive Narratological Analysis.
    • → [Applies] → ◻ TRANSFORM: Analyzing narrative features in terms of the cognitive processes they elicit or rely upon in the reader/viewer.
      • → [Operates On] → ◻ PATCH: Specific narrative structures, linguistic cues, character portrayals, plot developments.
      • → [Produces] → ◻ INVARIANT: Underlying cognitive strategies for narrative comprehension (e.g., script activation, inference generation, mental model construction); core components of "story logic."
      • → [Requires] → ◻ CONTRACTION: Abstracting from specific story content to the general cognitive operations involved in processing it.
    • → [Constructs] → ◻ REPRESENTATION: A model of narrative understanding grounded in cognitive science principles, applicable across media.
    • → [Optimizes] → ◻ RECURSION: Applying the cognitive framework to increasingly complex or unconventional narratives and different media platforms.
      • → [Embeds] → ◻ MODEL: Cognitive models of memory, inference, schema theory; linguistic pragmatics; narratological frameworks.
      • → [Projects] → ◻ METRIC: Cognitive plausibility, explanatory power for comprehension processes, coherence of the constructed storyworld (inferential).
      • → [Seeks] → ◻ FIXED POINT: A comprehensive cognitive theory of narrative capable of explaining how humans make sense of stories.
  • ◻ METAMODEL: Cognitive Theory of Narrative. Governs the analysis of story structures and comprehension. Binds Narrative Discourse/Structures DOMAIN ↔ Cognitive Processing/Mental Models CODMAIN.
  • Distinctions & Crossings:
    • ◻ MODEL (Cognitive model of the storyworld) | ◻ REALITY (The narrative text/stimulus OR the reality depicted) → ◻ MEASUREMENT ARTIFACT (The mental model constructed is an artifact of the interaction between text cues and the reader's cognitive processing/background knowledge).
    • ◻ TIME-LIKE (Unfolding of the plot, processing sequence) | ◻ SPACE-LIKE (The constructed storyworld, character relationships, spatial setting). Narrative comprehension involves building a space-like mental model from time-like discursive input.
    • ◻ CONTINUOUS (Implied storyworld, flow of events) | ◻ DISCRETE (Narrative cues, sentences, shots, inferences) → ◻ SAMPLING THRESHOLD (Cognition uses discrete cues to construct a representation of the continuous storyworld).
  • Operations: Observe narrative structures and infer comprehension processes. Encode narrative elements based on their cognitive function. Convolve textual cues (PATCH) with cognitive schemas (KERNEL) to generate inferences (FEATURE). Entangle discourse structure (FEATURE) with mental model construction (FEATURE). Tries not to Forget the active cognitive role of the reader/viewer.
