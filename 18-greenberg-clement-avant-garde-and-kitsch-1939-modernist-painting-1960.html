Greenberg Clement Avant Garde And Kitsch 1939 Modernist Painting 1960

1. Problem Statement

  1. Greenberg, Clement - “Avant-garde and Kitsch” (1939) & “Modernist Painting” (1960)

Focused Problem Statement: Explaining the success of Abstract Expressionism (L1 Artifact) simply through artist biography or general post-war angst obscures the specific formalist logic championed by Greenberg (L3 Framing). His model posits the paintings' value lies in fulfilling a historical imperative towards medium specificity (L0 Material Reality/History), a purification interpreted by critics/collectors (L2) as resisting kitsch, reflecting latent assumptions about aesthetic purity and avant-garde roles (L4).

2. Core Model

18. Greenberg, Clement - “Avant-garde and Kitsch” / “Modernist Painting” (1939 / 1960)

  • MODEL: Argues that true avant-garde art maintains high culture standards against the debased, formulaic "kitsch" produced for mass consumption; and that Modernist painting progressively purifies itself by emphasizing properties unique to the medium itself, particularly flatness.
  • Index:
    • ◻ Art → ◻ Distinction between Avant-Garde & Kitsch
    • ◻ Avant-Garde → ◻ High Culture / Formal Innovation
    • ◻ Kitsch → ◻ Mass Consumption / Formulaic / Debased
    • ◻ Modernist Painting → ◻ Self-Purification → ◻ Emphasis on Medium-Specific Properties (Flatness)

4. Spatiotemporal Framework

18. Greenberg, Clement - “Avant-garde and Kitsch” (1939) & “Modernist Painting” (1960)

  • ◻ SPATIOTEMPORAL UNIVERSE: History of Western art, particularly from the mid-19th century through modernism.
    • → [Contains] → ◻ FIELD: The production and reception of visual art, distinguishing between high art (avant-garde) and mass culture (kitsch), and the internal logic of modernist art.
      • → [Has] → ◻ DOMAIN: Artworks, artistic techniques, critical discourse, socio-economic conditions (patronage, market) × Historical time (Industrial Revolution to mid-20th C).
      • → [Projects] → ◻ SIGNAL: Formal qualities of artworks (flatness, abstraction, color, line), stylistic categories, critical judgments of value. (ℝᵏ representing formal properties, degree of abstraction, medium specificity).
      • → [Admits] → ◻ PERTURBATION: Emergence of new artistic styles, challenges to avant-garde principles, changing tastes of elites and masses.
    • → [Observed Through] → ◻ WINDOW: Formalist criticism, historical determinism (influenced by Marxism, but focused on culture), belief in aesthetic purity and quality.
      • → [Defines] → ◻ OBSERVATION SCALE: Focus on the internal formal logic and historical trajectory of art, especially painting. Evaluation based on medium specificity.
      • → [Delimits] → ◻ CONTEXT BOUNDARY: Privileges specific lineage of modern art (Manet to Abstract Expressionism), sharply separates avant-garde from kitsch, downplays social content unless reflected in form.
      • → [Generates] → ◻ SAMPLING LATTICE: Analysis of specific modernist paintings, comparison of avant-garde techniques vs. kitsch formulas.
    • → [Manifests As] → ◻ PHENOMENON: Avant-Garde vs. Kitsch & Modernist Self-Criticism – The historical necessity for a conscious avant-garde to sustain high culture against the corrosive effects of industrialized mass culture (kitsch). Modernist art progresses through a process of self-criticism, progressively purifying itself by emphasizing qualities unique to its specific medium (e.g., flatness in painting).
  • ◻ ENGINE: Formalist Analysis & Historical Teleology.
    • → [Applies] → ◻ TRANSFORM: Evaluating artworks based on their formal innovation, contribution to the medium's self-definition, and distance from kitsch.
      • → [Operates On] → ◻ PATCH: Specific artworks or artistic styles.
      • → [Produces] → ◻ INVARIANT: The principle of medium specificity; the historical logic driving modernism towards abstraction/flatness; the criteria distinguishing authentic art from kitsch.
      • → [Requires] → ◻ CONTRACTION: Focusing exclusively on formal properties and their relation to the medium, bracketing narrative, representation (as non-essential), and external social factors.
    • → [Constructs] → ◻ REPRESENTATION: A hierarchical model of culture (avant-garde vs. kitsch) and a teleological history of modernist painting.
    • → [Optimizes] → ◻ RECURSION: Applying the formalist criteria historically to trace the "correct" lineage of modernist development.
      • → [Embeds] → ◻ MODEL: Kantian aesthetics (disinterestedness, form), theories of historical progress, medium specificity.
      • → [Projects] → ◻ METRIC: Degree of formal purity, flatness, self-reflexivity, aesthetic quality (hierarchical judgment).
      • → [Seeks] → ◻ FIXED POINT: The ultimate realization of a medium's essential properties (e.g., pure abstraction/flatness for painting).
  • ◻ METAMODEL: Formalist Theory of Modern Art History. Governs aesthetic judgment and historical interpretation. Binds Artistic Medium Properties DOMAIN ↔ Aesthetic Value/Historical Significance CODMAIN.
  • Distinctions & Crossings:
    • ◻ MODEL (Formalist theory/historical narrative) | ◻ REALITY (Diverse art practices/social contexts) → ◻ MEASUREMENT ARTIFACT (The model elevates certain artworks fitting its criteria and dismisses others, creating a selective historical artifact).
    • ◻ TIME-LIKE (Historical progression of modernism) is central. SPACE-LIKE (The essential properties of the medium, e.g., the flat surface). Modernism is seen as time uncovering the essential space of the medium.
    • ◻ CONTINUOUS (History of art) | ◻ DISCRETE (Avant-garde vs. Kitsch categories, stages of modernist development) → ◻ SAMPLING THRESHOLD (Imposes sharp discrete categories onto the more continuous field of cultural production).
  • Operations: Observe artworks through a formalist lens. Encode them based on medium specificity and relation to kitsch. Convolve artworks (PATCH) with historical context (KERNEL) to place them in the modernist teleology. Entangle formal purity (FEATURE) with aesthetic value (FEATURE). Intentionally Forgets aspects deemed non-essential to the medium.
